PREFACE

  [[the expression “South African Human Rights Commission” substituted for the expression “Human Rights Commission”, wherever it occurred, by section 4 of the {{>/akn/za/act/1998/constitution-second-amendment Constitution Second Amendment Act, 1998}}]]

  LONGTITLE To introduce a new Constitution for the Republic of South Africa and to provide for matters incidental thereto.



PREAMBLE

  We, the people of South Africa,

  Recognise the injustices of our past;

  Honour those who suffered for justice and freedom in our land;

  Respect those who have worked to build and develop our country; and

  Believe that South Africa belongs to all who live in it, united in our diversity.

  We therefore, through our freely elected representatives, adopt this Constitution as the supreme law of the Republic so as to -

  Heal the divisions of the past and establish a society based on democratic values, social justice and fundamental human rights;

  Lay the foundations for a democratic and open society in which government is based on the will of the people and every citizen is equally protected by law;

  Improve the quality of life of all citizens and free the potential of each person; and

  Build a united and democratic South Africa able to take its rightful place as a sovereign state in the family of nations.

  May God protect our people.

  Nkosi Sikelel' iAfrika. Morena boloka setjhaba sa heso.

  God seën Suid-Afrika. God bless South Africa.

  Mudzimu fhatutshedza Afurika. Hosi katekisa Afrika.

BODY

CHAP 1 - Founding provisions

  SEC 1. - Republic of South Africa

    ITEMS
      The Republic of South Africa is one, sovereign, democratic state founded on the following values:

      ITEM (a)
        Human dignity, the achievement of equality and the advancement of human rights and freedoms.

      ITEM (b)
        Non-racialism and non-sexism.

      ITEM (c)
        Supremacy of the constitution and the rule of law.

      ITEM (d)
        Universal adult suffrage, a national common voters roll, regular elections and a multi-party system of democratic government, to ensure accountability, responsiveness and openness.

  SEC 2. - Supremacy of Constitution

    This Constitution is the supreme law of the Republic; law or conduct inconsistent with it is invalid, and the obligations imposed by it must be fulfilled.

  SEC 3. - Citizenship

    SUBSEC (1)

      There is a common South African Citizenship.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        All citizens are -

        ITEM (a)
          equally entitled to the rights, privileges and benefits of citizenship; and

        ITEM (b)
          equally subject to the duties and responsibilities of citizenship.

    SUBSEC (3)

      National legislation must provide for the acquisition, loss and restoration of citizenship.

  SEC 4. - National anthem

    The national anthem of the Republic is determined by the President by proclamation.

  SEC 5. - National flag

    The national flag of the Republic is black, gold, green, white, red and blue, as described and sketched in {{>#att_1 Schedule 1}}.

  SEC 6. - Languages

    SUBSEC (1)

      The official languages of the Republic are Sepedi, Sesotho, Setswana, siSwati, Tshivenda, Xitsonga, Afrikaans, English, isiNdebele, isiXhosa and isiZulu.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Recognising the historically diminished use and status of the indigenous languages of our people, the state must take practical and positive measures to elevate the status and advance the use of these languages.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          The national government and provincial governments may use any particular official languages for the purposes of government, taking into account usage, practicality, expense, regional circumstances and the balance of the needs and preferences of the population as a whole or in the province concerned; but the national government and each provincial government must use at least two official languages.

        ITEM (b)
          Municipalities must take into account the language usage and preferences of their residents.

    SUBSEC (4)

      The national government and provincial governments, by legislative and other measures, must regulate and monitor their use of official languages. Without detracting from the provisions of subsection (2), all official languages must enjoy parity of esteem and must be treated equitably.

    SUBSEC (5)

      ITEMS
        A Pan South African Language Board established by national legislation must -

        ITEM (a)
          ITEMS
            promote, and create conditions for, the development and use of -

            ITEM (i)
              all official languages;

            ITEM (ii)
              the Khoi, Nama and San languages; and

            ITEM (iii)
              sign language; and

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            promote and ensure respect for -

            ITEM (i)
              all languages commonly used by communities in South Africa, including German, Greek, Gujarati, Hindi, Portuguese, Tamil, Telegu and Urdu; and

            ITEM (ii)
              Arabic, Hebrew, Sanskrit and other languages used for religious purposes in South Africa.

CHAP 2 - Bill of Rights

  SEC 7. - Rights

    SUBSEC (1)

      This Bill of Rights is a cornerstone of democracy in South Africa. It enshrines the rights of all people in our country and affirms the democratic values of human dignity, equality and freedom.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The state must respect, protect, promote and fulfil the rights in the Bill of Rights.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The rights in the Bill of Rights are subject to the limitations contained or referred to in {{>#sec_36 section 36}}, or elsewhere in the Bill.

  SEC 8. - Application

    SUBSEC (1)

      The Bill of Rights applies to all law, and binds the legislature, the executive, the judiciary and all organs of state.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A provision of the Bill of Rights binds a natural or a juristic person if, and to the extent that, it is applicable, taking into account the nature of the right and the nature of any duty imposed by the right.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        When applying a provision of the Bill of Rights to a natural or juristic person in terms of subsection (2), a court -

        ITEM (a)
          in order to give effect to a right in the Bill, must apply, or if necessary develop, the common law to the extent that legislation does not give effect to that right; and

        ITEM (b)
          may develop rules of the common law to limit the right, provided that the limitation is in accordance with {{>#sec_36 section 36}}(1).

    SUBSEC (4)

      A juristic person is entitled to the rights in the Bill of Rights to the extent required by the nature of the rights and the nature of that juristic person.

  SEC 9. - Equality

    SUBSEC (1)

      Everyone is equal before the law and has the right to equal protection and benefit of the law.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Equality includes the full and equal enjoyment of all rights and freedoms. To promote the achievement of equality, legislative and other measures designed to protect or advance persons, or categories of persons, disadvantaged by unfair discrimination may be taken.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The state may not unfairly discriminate directly or indirectly against anyone on one or more grounds, including race, gender, sex, pregnancy, marital status, ethnic or social origin, colour, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, culture, language and birth.

    SUBSEC (4)

      No person may unfairly discriminate directly or indirectly against anyone on one or more grounds in terms of subsection (3). National legislation must be enacted to prevent or prohibit unfair discrimination.

    SUBSEC (5)

      Discrimination on one or more of the grounds listed in subsection (3) is unfair unless it is established that the discrimination is fair.

  SEC 10. - Human dignity

    Everyone has inherent dignity and the right to have their dignity respected and protected.

  SEC 11. - Life

    Everyone has the right to life.

  SEC 12. - Freedom and security of the person

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Everyone has the right to freedom and security of the person, which includes the right -

        ITEM (a)
          not to be deprived of freedom arbitrarily or without just cause;

        ITEM (b)
          not to be detained without trial;

        ITEM (c)
          to be free from all forms of violence from either public or private sources;

        ITEM (d)
          not to be tortured in any way; and

        ITEM (e)
          not to be treated or punished in a cruel, inhuman or degrading way.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Everyone has the right to bodily and psychological integrity, which includes the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to make decisions concerning reproduction;

        ITEM (b)
          to security in and control over their body; and

        ITEM (c)
          not to be subjected to medical or scientific experiments without their informed consent.

  SEC 13. - Slavery, servitude and forced labour

    No one may be subjected to slavery, servitude or forced labour.

  SEC 14. - Privacy

    ITEMS
      Everyone has the right to privacy, which includes the right not to have -

      ITEM (a)
        their person or home searched;

      ITEM (b)
        their property searched;

      ITEM (c)
        their possessions seized; or

      ITEM (d)
        the privacy of their communications infringed.

  SEC 15. - Freedom of religion, belief and opinion

    SUBSEC (1)

      Everyone has the right to freedom of conscience, religion, thought, belief and opinion.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Religious observances may be conducted at state or state aided institutions, provided that -

        ITEM (a)
          those observances follow rules made by the appropriate public authorities;

        ITEM (b)
          they are conducted on an equitable basis; and

        ITEM (c)
          attendance at them is free and voluntary.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          ITEMS
            This section does not prevent legislation recognising -

            ITEM (i)
              marriages concluded under any tradition, or a system of religious, personal or family law; or

            ITEM (ii)
              systems of personal and family law under any tradition, or adhered to by persons professing a particular religion.

        ITEM (b)
          Recognition in terms of paragraph (a) must be consistent with this section and the other provisions of the Constitution.

  SEC 16. - Freedom of expression

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Everyone has the right to freedom of expression, which includes -

        ITEM (a)
          freedom of the press and other media;

        ITEM (b)
          freedom to receive or impart information or ideas;

        ITEM (c)
          freedom of artistic creativity; and

        ITEM (d)
          academic freedom and freedom of scientific research.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        The right in subsection (1) does not extend to -

        ITEM (a)
          propaganda for war;

        ITEM (b)
          incitement of imminent violence; or

        ITEM (c)
          advocacy of hatred that is based on race, ethnicity, gender or religion, and that constitutes incitement to cause harm.

  SEC 17. - Assembly, demonstration, picket and petition

    Everyone has the right, peacefully and unarmed, to assemble, to demonstrate, to picket and to present petitions.

  SEC 18. - Freedom of association

    Everyone has the right to freedom of association.

  SEC 19. - Political rights

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Every citizen is free to make political choices, which includes the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to form a political party;

        ITEM (b)
          to participate in the activities of, or recruit members for, a political party; and

        ITEM (c)
          to campaign for a political party or cause.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Every citizen has the right to free, fair and regular elections for any legislative body established in terms of the Constitution.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        Every adult citizen has the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to vote in elections for any legislative body established in terms of the Constitution, and to do so in secret; and

        ITEM (b)
          to stand for public office and, if elected, to hold office.

  SEC 20. - Citizenship

    No citizen may be deprived of citizenship.

  SEC 21. - Freedom of movement and residence

    SUBSEC (1)

      Everyone has the right to freedom of movement.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Everyone has the right to leave the Republic.

    SUBSEC (3)

      Every citizen has the right to enter, to remain in and to reside anywhere in, the Republic.

    SUBSEC (4)

      Every citizen has the right to a passport.

  SEC 22. - Freedom of trade, occupation and profession

    Every citizen has the right to choose their trade, occupation or profession freely. The practice of a trade, occupation or profession may be regulated by law.

  SEC 23. - Labour relations

    SUBSEC (1)

      Everyone has the right to fair labour practices.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Every worker has the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to form and join a trade union;

        ITEM (b)
          to participate in the activities and programmes of a trade union; and

        ITEM (c)
          to strike.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        Every employer has the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to form and join an employers' organisation; and

        ITEM (b)
          to participate in the activities and programmes of an employers' organisation.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        Every trade union and every employers' organisation has the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to determine its own administration, programmes and activities;

        ITEM (b)
          to organise; and

        ITEM (c)
          to form and join a federation.

    SUBSEC (5)

      Every trade union, employers' organisation and employer has the right to engage in collective bargaining. National legislation may be enacted to regulate collective bargaining. To the extent that the legislation may limit a right in this Chapter, the limitation must comply with {{>#sec_36 section 36}}(1).

    SUBSEC (6)

      National legislation may recognise union security arrangements contained in collective agreements. To the extent that the legislation may limit a right in this Chapter, the limitation must comply with {{>#sec_36 section 36}}(1).

  SEC 24. - Environment

    ITEMS
      Everyone has the right -

      ITEM (a)
        to an environment that is not harmful to their health or well-being; and

      ITEM (b)
        ITEMS
          to have the environment protected, for the benefit of present and future generations, through reasonable legislative and other measures that -

          ITEM (i)
            prevent pollution and ecological degradation;

          ITEM (ii)
            promote conservation; and

          ITEM (iii)
            secure ecologically sustainable development and use of natural resources while promoting justifiable economic and social development.

  SEC 25. - Property

    SUBSEC (1)

      No one may be deprived of property except in terms of law of general application, and no law may permit arbitrary deprivation of property.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Property may be expropriated only in terms of law of general application -

        ITEM (a)
          for a public purpose or in the public interest; and

        ITEM (b)
          subject to compensation, the amount of which and the time and manner of payment of which have either been agreed to by those affected or decided or approved by a court.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        The amount of the compensation and the time and manner of payment must be just and equitable, reflecting an equitable balance between the public interest and the interests of those affected, having regard to all relevant circumstances, including -

        ITEM (a)
          the current use of the property;

        ITEM (b)
          the history of the acquisition and use of the property;

        ITEM (c)
          the market value of the property;

        ITEM (d)
          the extent of direct state investment and subsidy in the acquisition and beneficial capital improvement of the property; and

        ITEM (e)
          the purpose of the expropriation.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        For the purposes of this section -

        ITEM (a)
          the public interest includes the nation's commitment to land reform, and to reforms to bring about equitable access to all South Africa's natural resources; and

        ITEM (b)
          property is not limited to land.

    SUBSEC (5)

      The state must take reasonable legislative and other measures, within its available resources, to foster conditions which enable citizens to gain access to land on an equitable basis.

    SUBSEC (6)

      A person or community whose tenure of land is legally insecure as a result of past racially discriminatory laws or practices is entitled, to the extent provided by an Act of Parliament, either to tenure which is legally secure or to comparable redress.

    SUBSEC (7)

      A person or community dispossessed of property after 19 June 1913 as a result of past racially discriminatory laws or practices is entitled, to the extent provided by an Act of Parliament, either to restitution of that property or to equitable redress.

    SUBSEC (8)

      No provision of this section may impede the state from taking legislative and other measures to achieve land, water and related reform, in order to redress the results of past racial discrimination, provided that any departure from the provisions of this section is in accordance with the provisions of {{>#sec_36 section 36}}(1).

    SUBSEC (9)

      Parliament must enact the legislation referred to in subsection (6).

  SEC 26. - Housing

    SUBSEC (1)

      Everyone has the right to have access to adequate housing.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The state must take reasonable legislative and other measures, within its available resources, to achieve the progressive realisation of this right.

    SUBSEC (3)

      No one may be evicted from their home, or have their home demolished, without an order of court made after considering all the relevant circumstances. No legislation may permit arbitrary evictions.

  SEC 27. - Health care, food, water and social security

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Everyone has the right to have access to -

        ITEM (a)
          health care services, including reproductive health care;

        ITEM (b)
          sufficient food and water; and

        ITEM (c)
          social security, including, if they are unable to support themselves and their dependants, appropriate social assistance.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The state must take reasonable legislative and other measures, within its available resources, to achieve the progressive realisation of each of these rights.

    SUBSEC (3)

      No one may be refused emergency medical treatment.

  SEC 28. - Children

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Every child has the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to a name and a nationality from birth;

        ITEM (b)
          to family care or parental care, or to appropriate alternative care when removed from the family environment;

        ITEM (c)
          to basic nutrition, shelter, basic health care services and social services;

        ITEM (d)
          to be protected from maltreatment, neglect, abuse or degradation;

        ITEM (e)
          to be protected from exploitative labour practices;

        ITEM (f)
          ITEMS
            not to be required or permitted to perform work or provide services that -

            ITEM (i)
              are inappropriate for a person of that child's age; or

            ITEM (ii)
              place at risk the child's well-being, education, physical or mental health or spiritual, moral or social development;

        ITEM (g)
          ITEMS
            not to be detained except as a measure of last resort, in which case, in addition to the rights a child enjoys under sections {{>#sec_12 12}} and {{>#sec_35 35}}, the child may be detained only for the shortest appropriate period of time, and has the right to be -

            ITEM (i)
              kept separately from detained persons over the age of 18 years; and

            ITEM (ii)
              treated in a manner, and kept in conditions, that take account of the child's age;

        ITEM (h)
          to have a legal practitioner assigned to the child by the state, and at state expense, in civil proceedings affecting the child, if substantial injustice would otherwise result; and

        ITEM (i)
          not to be used directly in armed conflict, and to be protected in times of armed conflict.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A child's best interests are of paramount importance in every matter concerning the child.

    SUBSEC (3)

      In this section "child" means a person under the age of 18 years.

  SEC 29. - Education

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Everyone has the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to a basic education, including adult basic education; and

        ITEM (b)
          to further education, which the state, through reasonable measures, must make progressively available and accessible.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Everyone has the right to receive education in the official language or languages of their choice in public educational institutions where that education is reasonably practicable. In order to ensure the effective access to, and implementation of, this right, the state must consider all reasonable educational alternatives, including single medium institutions, taking into account -

        ITEM (a)
          equity;

        ITEM (b)
          practicability; and

        ITEM (c)
          the need to redress the results of past racially discriminatory laws and practices.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        Everyone has the right to establish and maintain, at their own expense, independent educational institutions that -

        ITEM (a)
          do not discriminate on the basis of race;

        ITEM (b)
          are registered with the state; and

        ITEM (c)
          maintain standards that are not inferior to standards at comparable public educational institutions.

    SUBSEC (4)

      Subsection (3) does not preclude state subsidies for independent educational institutions.

  SEC 30. - Language and culture

    Everyone has the right to use the language and to participate in the cultural life of their choice, but no one exercising these rights may do so in a manner inconsistent with any provision of the Bill of Rights.

  SEC 31. - Cultural, religious and linguistic communities

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Persons belonging to a cultural, religious or linguistic community may not be denied the right, with other members of that community -

        ITEM (a)
          to enjoy their culture, practise their religion and use their language; and

        ITEM (b)
          to form, join and maintain cultural, religious and linguistic associations and other organs of civil society.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The rights in subsection (1) may not be exercised in a manner inconsistent with any provision of the Bill of Rights.

  SEC 32. - Access to information

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Everyone has the right of access to -

        ITEM (a)
          any information held by the state; and

        ITEM (b)
          any information that is held by another person and that is required for the exercise or protection of any rights.

    SUBSEC (2)

      National legislation must be enacted to give effect to this right, and may provide for reasonable measures to alleviate the administrative and financial burden on the state.

  SEC 33. - Just administrative action

    SUBSEC (1)

      Everyone has the right to administrative action that is lawful, reasonable and procedurally fair.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Everyone whose rights have been adversely affected by administrative action has the right to be given written reasons.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        National legislation must be enacted to give effect to these rights, and must -

        ITEM (a)
          provide for the review of administrative action by a court or, where appropriate, an independent and impartial tribunal;

        ITEM (b)
          impose a duty on the state to give effect to the rights in subsections (1) and (2); and

        ITEM (c)
          promote an efficient administration.

  SEC 34. - Access to courts

    Everyone has the right to have any dispute that can be resolved by the application of law decided in a fair public hearing before a court or, where appropriate, another independent and impartial tribunal or forum.

  SEC 35. - Arrested, detained and accused persons

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Everyone who is arrested for allegedly committing an offence has the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to remain silent;

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            to be informed promptly -

            ITEM (i)
              of the right to remain silent; and

            ITEM (ii)
              of the consequences of not remaining silent;

        ITEM (c)
          not to be compelled to make any confession or admission that could be used in evidence against that person;

        ITEM (d)
          ITEMS
            to be brought before a court as soon as reasonably possible, but not later than -

            ITEM (i)
              48 hours after the arrest; or

            ITEM (ii)
              the end of the first court day after the expiry of the 48 hours, if the 48 hours expire outside ordinary court hours or on a day which is not an ordinary court day;

        ITEM (e)
          at the first court appearance after being arrested, to be charged or to be informed of the reason for the detention to continue, or to be released; and

        ITEM (f)
          to be released from detention if the interests of justice permit, subject to reasonable conditions.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Everyone who is detained, including every sentenced prisoner, has the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to be informed promptly of the reason for being detained;

        ITEM (b)
          to choose, and to consult with, a legal practitioner, and to be informed of this right promptly;

        ITEM (c)
          to have a legal practitioner assigned to the detained person by the state and at state expense, if substantial injustice would otherwise result, and to be informed of this right promptly;

        ITEM (d)
          to challenge the lawfulness of the detention in person before a court and, if the detention is unlawful, to be released;

        ITEM (e)
          to conditions of detention that are consistent with human dignity, including at least exercise and the provision, at state expense, of adequate accommodation, nutrition, reading material and medical treatment; and

        ITEM (f)
          ITEMS
            to communicate with, and be visited by, that person's -

            ITEM (i)
              spouse or partner;

            ITEM (ii)
              next of kin;

            ITEM (iii)
              chosen religious counsellor; and

            ITEM (iv)
              chosen medical practitioner.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        Every accused person has a right to a fair trial, which includes the right -

        ITEM (a)
          to be informed of the charge with sufficient detail to answer it;

        ITEM (b)
          to have adequate time and facilities to prepare a defence;

        ITEM (c)
          to a public trial before an ordinary court;

        ITEM (d)
          to have their trial begin and conclude without unreasonable delay;

        ITEM (e)
          to be present when being tried;

        ITEM (f)
          to choose, and be represented by, a legal practitioner, and to be informed of this right promptly;

        ITEM (g)
          to have a legal practitioner assigned to the accused person by the state and at state expense, if substantial injustice would otherwise result, and to be informed of this right promptly;

        ITEM (h)
          to be presumed innocent, to remain silent, and not to testify during the proceedings;

        ITEM (i)
          to adduce and challenge evidence;

        ITEM (j)
          not to be compelled to give self-incriminating evidence;

        ITEM (k)
          to be tried in a language that the accused person understands or, if that is not practicable, to have the proceedings interpreted in that language;

        ITEM (l)
          not to be convicted for an act or omission that was not an offence under either national or international law at the time it was committed or omitted;

        ITEM (m)
          not to be tried for an offence in respect of an act or omission for which that person has previously been either acquitted or convicted;

        ITEM (n)
          to the benefit of the least severe of the prescribed punishments if the prescribed punishment for the offence has been changed between the time that the offence was committed and the time of sentencing; and

        ITEM (o)
          of appeal to, or review by, a higher court.

    SUBSEC (4)

      Whenever this section requires information to be given to a person, that information must be given in a language that the person understands.

    SUBSEC (5)

      Evidence obtained in a manner that violates any right in the Bill of Rights must be excluded if the admission of that evidence would render the trial unfair or otherwise be detrimental to the administration of justice.

  SEC 36. - Limitation of rights

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        The rights in the Bill of Rights may be limited only in terms of law of general application to the extent that the limitation is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality and freedom, taking into account all relevant factors, including -

        ITEM (a)
          the nature of the right;

        ITEM (b)
          the importance of the purpose of the limitation;

        ITEM (c)
          the nature and extent of the limitation;

        ITEM (d)
          the relation between the limitation and its purpose; and

        ITEM (e)
          less restrictive means to achieve the purpose.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Except as provided in subsection (1) or in any other provision of the Constitution, no law may limit any right entrenched in the Bill of Rights.

  SEC 37. - States of emergency

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        A state of emergency may be declared only in terms of an Act of Parliament, and only when -

        ITEM (a)
          the life of the nation is threatened by war, invasion, general insurrection, disorder, natural disaster or other public emergency; and

        ITEM (b)
          the declaration is necessary to restore peace and order.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        A declaration of a state of emergency, and any legislation enacted or other action taken in consequence of that declaration, may be effective only -

        ITEM (a)
          prospectively; and

        ITEM (b)
          for no more than 21 days from the date of the declaration, unless the National Assembly resolves to extend the declaration. The Assembly may extend a declaration of a state of emergency for no more than three months at a time. The first extension of the state of emergency must be by a resolution adopted with a supporting vote of a majority of the members of the Assembly. Any subsequent extension must be by a resolution adopted with a supporting vote of at least 60 per cent of the members of the Assembly. A resolution in terms of this paragraph may be adopted only following a public debate in the Assembly.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        Any competent court may decide on the validity of -

        ITEM (a)
          a declaration of a state of emergency;

        ITEM (b)
          any extension of a declaration of a state of emergency; or

        ITEM (c)
          any legislation enacted, or other action taken, in consequence of a declaration of a state of emergency.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        Any legislation enacted in consequence of a declaration of a state of emergency may derogate from the Bill of Rights only to the extent that -

        ITEM (a)
          the derogation is strictly required by the emergency; and

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            the legislation -

            ITEM (i)
              is consistent with the Republic's obligations under international law applicable to states of emergency;

            ITEM (ii)
              conforms to subsection (5); and

            ITEM (iii)
              is published in the national //Government Gazette// as soon as reasonably possible after being enacted.

    SUBSEC (5)

      ITEMS
        No Act of Parliament that authorises a declaration of a state of emergency, and no legislation enacted or other action taken in consequence of a declaration, may permit or authorise -

        ITEM (a)
          indemnifying the state, or any person, in respect of any unlawful act;

        ITEM (b)
          any derogation from this section; or

        ITEM (c)
          any derogation from a section mentioned in column 1 of the Table of Non-Derogable Rights, to the extent indicated opposite that section in column 3 of the Table.

    SUBSEC (6)

      ITEMS
        Whenever anyone is detained without trial in consequence of a derogation of rights resulting from a declaration of a state of emergency, the following conditions must be observed:

        ITEM (a)
          An adult family member or friend of the detainee must be contacted as soon as reasonably possible, and informed that the person has been detained.

        ITEM (b)
          A notice must be published in the national //Government Gazette// within five days of the person being detained, stating the detainee's name and place of detention and referring to the emergency measure in terms of which that person has been detained.

        ITEM (c)
          The detainee must be allowed to choose, and be visited at any reasonable time by, a medical practitioner.

        ITEM (d)
          The detainee must be allowed to choose, and be visited at any reasonable time by, a legal representative.

        ITEM (e)
          A court must review the detention as soon as reasonably possible, but no later than 10 days after the date the person was detained, and the court must release the detainee unless it is necessary to continue the detention to restore peace and order.

        ITEM (f)
          A detainee who is not released in terms of a review under paragraph (e), or who is not released in terms of a review under this paragraph, may apply to a court for a further review of the detention at any time after 10 days have passed since the previous review, and the court must release the detainee unless it is still necessary to continue the detention to restore peace and order.

        ITEM (g)
          The detainee must be allowed to appear in person before any court considering the detention, to be represented by a legal practitioner at those hearings, and to make representations against continued detention.

        ITEM (h)
          The state must present written reasons to the court to justify the continued detention of the detainee, and must give a copy of those reasons to the detainee at least two days before the court reviews the detention.

    SUBSEC (7)

      If a court releases a detainee, that person may not be detained again on the same grounds unless the state first shows a court good cause for re-detaining that person.

    SUBSEC (8)

      Subsections (6) and (7) do not apply to persons who are not South African citizens and who are detained in consequence of an international armed conflict. Instead, the state must comply with the standards binding on the Republic under international humanitarian law in respect of the detention of such persons.

  SEC 38. - Enforcement of rights

    ITEMS
      Anyone listed in this section has the right to approach a competent court, alleging that a right in the Bill of Rights has been infringed or threatened, and the court may grant appropriate relief, including a declaration of rights. The persons who may approach a court are -

      ITEM (a)
        anyone acting in their own interest;

      ITEM (b)
        anyone acting on behalf of another person who cannot act in their own name;

      ITEM (c)
        anyone acting as a member of, or in the interest of, a group or class of persons;

      ITEM (d)
        anyone acting in the public interest; and

      ITEM (e)
        an association acting in the interest of its members.

  SEC 39. - Interpretation of Bill of Rights

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        When interpreting the Bill of Rights, a court, tribunal or forum -

        ITEM (a)
          must promote the values that underlie an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality and freedom;

        ITEM (b)
          must consider international law; and

        ITEM (c)
          may consider foreign law.

    SUBSEC (2)

      When interpreting any legislation, and when developing the common law or customary law, every court, tribunal or forum must promote the spirit, purport and objects of the Bill of Rights.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The Bill of Rights does not deny the existence of any other rights or freedoms that are recognised or conferred by common law, customary law or legislation, to the extent that they are consistent with the Bill.

CHAP 3 - Co-operative government

  SEC 40. - Government of the Republic

    SUBSEC (1)

      In the Republic, government is constituted as national, provincial and local spheres of government which are distinctive, interdependent and interrelated.

    SUBSEC (2)

      All spheres of government must observe and adhere to the principles in this Chapter and must conduct their activities within the parameters that the Chapter provides.

  SEC 41. - Principles of co-operative government and intergovernmental relations

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        All spheres of government and all organs of state within each sphere must -

        ITEM (a)
          preserve the peace, national unity and the indivisibility of the Republic;

        ITEM (b)
          secure the well-being of the people of the Republic;

        ITEM (c)
          provide effective, transparent, accountable and coherent government for the Republic as a whole;

        ITEM (d)
          be loyal to the Constitution, the Republic and its people;

        ITEM (e)
          respect the constitutional status, institutions, powers and functions of government in the other spheres;

        ITEM (f)
          not assume any power or function except those conferred on them in terms of the Constitution;

        ITEM (g)
          exercise their powers and perform their functions in a manner that does not encroach on the geographical, functional or institutional integrity of government in another sphere; and

        ITEM (h)
          ITEMS
            co-operate with one another in mutual trust and good faith by -

            ITEM (i)
              fostering friendly relations;

            ITEM (ii)
              assisting and supporting one another;

            ITEM (iii)
              informing one another of, and consulting one another on, matters of common interest;

            ITEM (iv)
              co-ordinating their actions and legislation with one another;

            ITEM (v)
              adhering to agreed procedures; and

            ITEM (vi)
              avoiding legal proceedings against one another.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        An Act of Parliament must -

        ITEM (a)
          establish or provide for structures and institutions to promote and facilitate intergovernmental relations; and

        ITEM (b)
          provide for appropriate mechanisms and procedures to facilitate settlement of intergovernmental disputes.

    SUBSEC (3)

      An organ of state involved in an intergovernmental dispute must make every reasonable effort to settle the dispute by means of mechanisms and procedures provided for that purpose, and must exhaust all other remedies before it approaches a court to resolve the dispute.

    SUBSEC (4)

      If a court is not satisfied that the requirements of subsection (3) have been met, it may refer a dispute back to the organs of state involved.

CHAP 4 - Parliament

  SEC 42. - Composition of Parliament

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Parliament consists of -

        ITEM (a)
          the National Assembly; and

        ITEM (b)
          the National Council of Provinces.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The National Assembly and the National Council of Provinces participate in the legislative process in the manner set out in the Constitution.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The National Assembly is elected to represent the people and to ensure government by the people under the Constitution. It does this by choosing the President, by providing a national forum for public consideration of issues, by passing legislation and by scrutinizing and overseeing executive action.

    SUBSEC (4)

      The National Council of Provinces represents the provinces to ensure that provincial interests are taken into account in the national sphere of government. It does this mainly by participating in the national legislative process and by providing a national forum for public consideration of issues affecting the provinces.

    SUBSEC (5)

      The President may summon Parliament to an extraordinary sitting at any time to conduct special business.

    SUBSEC (6)

      The seat of Parliament is Cape Town, but an Act of Parliament enacted in accordance with {{>#sec_76 section 76}}(1) and (5) may determine that the seat of Parliament is elsewhere.

  SEC 43. - Legislative authority of the Republic

    ITEMS
      In the Republic, the legislative authority -

      ITEM (a)
        of the national sphere of government is vested in Parliament, as set out in {{>#sec_44 section 44}};

      ITEM (b)
        of the provincial sphere of government is vested in the provincial legislatures, as set out in {{>#sec_104 section 104}}; and

      ITEM (c)
        of the local sphere of government is vested in the Municipal Councils, as set out in {{>#sec_156 section 156}}.

  SEC 44. - National legislative authority

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        The national legislative authority as vested in Parliament -

        ITEM (a)
          ITEMS
            confers on the National Assembly the power -

            ITEM (i)
              to amend the Constitution;

            ITEM (ii)
              to pass legislation, with regard to any matter, including a matter within a functional area listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}}, but excluding, subject to subsection (2), a matter within a functional area listed in {{>#att_6 Schedule 5}}; and

            ITEM (iii)
              to assign any of its legislative powers, except the power to amend the Constitution, to any legislative body in another sphere of government; and

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            confers on the National Council of Provinces the power -

            ITEM (i)
              to participate in amending the Constitution in accordance with {{>#sec_74 section 74}};

            ITEM (ii)
              to pass, in accordance with {{>#sec_76 section 76}}, legislation with regard to any matter within a functional area listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}} and any other matter required by the Constitution to be passed in accordance with {{>#sec_76 section 76}}; and

            ITEM (iii)
              to consider, in accordance with {{>#sec_75 section 75}}, any other legislation passed by the National Assembly.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Parliament may intervene, by passing legislation in accordance with {{>#sec_76 section 76}}(1), with regard to a matter falling within a functional area listed in {{>#att_6 Schedule 5}}, when it is necessary -

        ITEM (a)
          to maintain national security;

        ITEM (b)
          to maintain economic unity;

        ITEM (c)
          to maintain essential national standards;

        ITEM (d)
          to establish minimum standards required for the rendering of services; or

        ITEM (e)
          to prevent unreasonable action taken by a province which is prejudicial to the interests of another province or to the country as a whole.

    SUBSEC (3)

      Legislation with regard to a matter that is reasonably necessary for, or incidental to, the effective exercise of a power concerning any matter listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}} is, for all purposes, legislation with regard to a matter listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}}.

    SUBSEC (4)

      When exercising its legislative authority, Parliament is bound only by the Constitution, and must act in accordance with, and within the limits of, the Constitution.

  SEC 45. - Joint rules and orders and joint committees

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        The National Assembly and the National Council of Provinces must establish a joint rules committee to make rules and orders concerning the joint business of the Assembly and Council, including rules and orders -

        ITEM (a)
          to determine procedures to facilitate the legislative process, including setting a time limit for completing any step in the process;

        ITEM (b)
          to establish joint committees composed of representatives from both the Assembly and the Council to consider and report on Bills envisaged in sections {{>#sec_74 74}} and {{>#sec_75 75}} that are referred to such a committee;

        ITEM (c)
          to establish a joint committee to review the Constitution at least annually; and

        ITEM (d)
          ITEMS
            to regulate the business of -

            ITEM (i)
              the joint rules committee;

            ITEM (ii)
              the Mediation Committee;

            ITEM (iii)
              the constitutional review committee; and

            ITEM (iv)
              any joint committees established in terms of paragraph (b).

    SUBSEC (2)

      Cabinet members, members of the National Assembly and delegates to the National Council of Provinces have the same privileges and immunities before a joint committee of the Assembly and the Council as they have before the Assembly or the Council.

  SUBPART - The National Assembly

    SEC 46. - Composition and election

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The National Assembly consists of no fewer than 350 and no more than 400 women and men elected as members in terms of an electoral system that -

          ITEM (a)
            is prescribed by national legislation;

          ITEM (b)
            is based on the national common voters roll;

          ITEM (c)
            provides for a minimum voting age of 18 years; and

          ITEM (d)
            results in general, in proportional representation.

      SUBSEC (2)

        An Act of Parliament must provide a formula for determining the number of members of the National Assembly.

    SEC 47. - Membership

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Every citizen who is qualified to vote for the National Assembly is eligible to be a member of the Assembly, except -

          ITEM (a)
            ITEMS
              anyone who is appointed by, or is in the service of, the state and receives remuneration for that appointment or service, other than -

              ITEM (i)
                the President, Deputy President, Ministers and Deputy Ministers; and

              ITEM (ii)
                other office-bearers whose functions are compatible with the functions of a member of the Assembly, and have been declared compatible with those functions by national legislation;

          ITEM (b)
            permanent delegates to the National Council of Provinces or members of a provincial legislature or a Municipal Council;

          ITEM (c)
            unrehabilitated insolvents;

          ITEM (d)
            anyone declared to be of unsound mind by a court of the Republic; or

          ITEM (e)
            anyone who, after this section took effect, is convicted of an offence and sentenced to more than 12 months' imprisonment without the option of a fine, either in the Republic, or outside the Republic if the conduct constituting the offence would have been an offence in the Republic, but no one may be regarded as having been sentenced until an appeal against the conviction or sentence has been determined, or until the time for an appeal has expired. A disqualification under this paragraph ends five years after the sentence has been completed.

      SUBSEC (2)

        A person who is not eligible to be a member of the National Assembly in terms of subsection (1)(a) or (b) may be a candidate for the Assembly, subject to any limits or conditions established by national legislation.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          A person loses membership of the National Assembly if that person—

          ITEM (a)
            ceases to be eligible; or

          ITEM (b)
            is absent from the Assembly without permission in circumstances for which the rules and orders of the Assembly prescribe loss of membership.

      SUBSEC (4)

        Vacancies in the National Assembly must be filled in terms of national legislation.

    SEC 48. - Oath or affirmation

      Before members of the National Assembly begin to perform their functions in the Assembly, they must swear or affirm faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

    SEC 49. - Duration of National Assembly

      SUBSEC (1)

        The National Assembly is elected for a term of five years.

      SUBSEC (2)

        If the National Assembly is dissolved in terms of {{>#sec_50 section 50}}, or when its term expires, the President, by proclamation, must call and set dates for an election, which must be held within 90 days of the date the Assembly was dissolved or its term expired.

      SUBSEC (3)

        If the result of an election of the National Assembly is not declared within the period established in terms of {{>#sec_190 section 190}}, or if an election is set aside by a court, the President, by proclamation, must call and set dates for another election, which must be held within 90 days of the expiry of that period or of the date on which the election was set aside.

      SUBSEC (4)

        The National Assembly remains competent to function from the time it is dissolved or its term expires, until the day before the first day of polling for the next Assembly.

    SEC 50. - Dissolution of National Assembly before expiry of its term

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The President must dissolve the National Assembly if -

          ITEM (a)
            the Assembly has adopted a resolution to dissolve with a supporting vote of a majority of its members; and

          ITEM (b)
            three years have passed since the Assembly was elected.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The Acting President must dissolve the National Assembly if -

          ITEM (a)
            there is a vacancy in the office of President; and

          ITEM (b)
            the Assembly fails to elect a new President within 30 days after the vacancy occurred.

    SEC 51. - Sittings and recess periods

      SUBSEC (1)

        After an election, the first sitting of the National Assembly must take place at a time and on a date determined by the President of the Constitutional Court, but not more than 14 days after the election result has been declared. The Assembly may determine the time and duration of its other sittings and its recess periods.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The President may summon the National Assembly to an extraordinary sitting at any time to conduct special business.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Sittings of the National Assembly are permitted at places other than the seat of Parliament only on the grounds of public interest, security or convenience, and if provided for in the rules and orders of the Assembly.

    SEC 52. - Speaker and Deputy Speaker

      SUBSEC (1)

        At the first sitting after its election, or when necessary to fill a vacancy, the National Assembly must elect a Speaker and a Deputy Speaker from among its members.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The President of the Constitutional Court must preside over the election of a Speaker, or designate another judge to do so. The Speaker presides over the election of a Deputy Speaker.

      SUBSEC (3)

        The procedure set out in Part A of {{>#att_4 Schedule 3}} applies to the election of the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker.

      SUBSEC (4)

        The National Assembly may remove the Speaker or Deputy Speaker from office by resolution. A majority of the members of the Assembly must be present when the resolution is adopted.

      SUBSEC (5)

        In terms of its rules and orders, the National Assembly may elect from among its members other presiding officers to assist the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker.

    SEC 53. - Decisions

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Except where the Constitution provides otherwise -

          ITEM (a)
            a majority of the members of the National Assembly must be present before a vote may be taken on a Bill or an amendment to a Bill;

          ITEM (b)
            at least one third of the members must be present before a vote may be taken on any other question before the Assembly; and

          ITEM (c)
            all questions before the Assembly are decided by a majority of the votes cast.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The member of the National Assembly presiding at a meeting of the Assembly has no deliberative vote, but -

          ITEM (a)
            must cast a deciding vote when there is an equal number of votes on each side of a question; and

          ITEM (b)
            may cast a deliberative vote when a question must be decided with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of the members of the Assembly.

    SEC 54. - Rights of certain Cabinet members in National Assembly

      The President and any member of the Cabinet who is not a member of the National Assembly may attend, and may speak in, the Assembly, but may not vote.

    SEC 55. - Powers of National Assembly

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          In exercising its legislative power, the National Assembly may -

          ITEM (a)
            consider, pass, amend or reject any legislation before the Assembly; and

          ITEM (b)
            initiate or prepare legislation, except money Bills.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The National Assembly must provide for mechanisms -

          ITEM (a)
            to ensure that all executive organs of state in the national sphere of government are accountable to it; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              to maintain oversight of -

              ITEM (i)
                the exercise of national executive authority, including the implementation of legislation; and

              ITEM (ii)
                any organ of state.

    SEC 56. - Evidence or information before National Assembly

      ITEMS
        The National Assembly or any of its committees may -

        ITEM (a)
          summon any person to appear before it to give evidence on oath or affirmation, or to produce documents;

        ITEM (b)
          require any person or institution to report to it;

        ITEM (c)
          compel, in terms of national legislation or the rules and orders, any person or institution to comply with a summons or requirement in terms of paragraph (a) or (b); and

        ITEM (d)
          receive petitions, representations or submissions from any interested persons or institutions.

    SEC 57. - Internal arrangements, proceedings and procedures of National Assembly

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The National Assembly may -

          ITEM (a)
            determine and control its internal arrangements, proceedings and procedures; and

          ITEM (b)
            make rules and orders concerning its business, with due regard to representative and participatory democracy, accountability, transparency and public involvement.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The rules and orders of the National Assembly must provide for -

          ITEM (a)
            the establishment, composition, powers, functions, procedures and duration of its committees;

          ITEM (b)
            the participation in the proceedings of the Assembly and its committees of minority parties represented in the Assembly, in a manner consistent with democracy;

          ITEM (c)
            financial and administrative assistance to each party represented in the Assembly in proportion to its representation, to enable the party and its leader to perform their functions in the Assembly effectively; and

          ITEM (d)
            the recognition of the leader of the largest opposition party in the Assembly as the Leader of the Opposition.

    SEC 58. - Privilege

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Cabinet members and members of the National Assembly -

          ITEM (a)
            have freedom of speech in the Assembly and in its committees, subject to its rules and orders; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              are not liable to civil or criminal proceedings, arrest, imprisonment or damages for -

              ITEM (i)
                anything that they have said in, produced before or submitted to the Assembly or any of its committees; or

              ITEM (ii)
                anything revealed as a result of anything that they have said in, produced before or submitted to the Assembly or any of its committees.

      SUBSEC (2)

        Other privileges and immunities of the National Assembly, Cabinet members and members of the Assembly may be prescribed by national legislation.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Salaries, allowances and benefits payable to members of the National Assembly are a direct charge against the National Revenue Fund.

    SEC 59. - Public access to and involvement in National Assembly

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The National Assembly must -

          ITEM (a)
            facilitate public involvement in the legislative and other processes of the Assembly and its committees; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              conduct its business in an open manner, and hold its sittings, and those of its committees, in public, but reasonable measures may be taken -

              ITEM (i)
                to regulate public access, including access of the media, to the Assembly and its committees; and

              ITEM (ii)
                to provide for the searching of any person and, where appropriate, the refusal of entry to, or the removal of, any person.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The National Assembly may not exclude the public, including the media, from a sitting of a committee unless it is reasonable and justifiable to do so in an open and democratic society.

  SUBPART - National Council of Provinces

    SEC 60. - Composition of National Council

      SUBSEC (1)

        The National Council of Provinces is composed of a single delegation from each province consisting of ten delegates.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The ten delegates are -

          ITEM (a)
            ITEMS
              four special delegates consisting of -

              ITEM (i)
                the Premier of the province or, if the Premier is not available, any member of the provincial legislature designated by the Premier either generally or for any specific business before the National Council of Provinces; and

              ITEM (ii)
                three other special delegates; and

          ITEM (b)
            six permanent delegates appointed in terms of {{>#sec_61 section 61}}(2).

      SUBSEC (3)

        The Premier of a province, or if the Premier is not available, a member of the province's delegation designated by the Premier, heads the delegation.

    SEC 61. - Allocation of delegates

      SUBSEC (1)

        Parties represented in a provincial legislature are entitled to delegates in the province's delegation in accordance with the formula set out in Part B of {{>#att_4 Schedule 3}}.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          Within 30 days after the result of an election of a provincial legislature is declared, the legislature must -

          ITEM (a)
            determine, in accordance with national legislation, how many of each party's delegates are to be permanent delegates and how many are to be special delegates; and

          ITEM (b)
            appoint the permanent delegates in accordance with the nominations of the parties.

      SUBSEC (3)

        The national legislation envisaged in subsection (2)(a) must ensure the participation of minority parties in both the permanent and special delegates' components of the delegation in a manner consistent with democracy.

      SUBSEC (4)

        The legislature, with the concurrence of the Premier and the leaders of the parties entitled to special delegates in the province's delegation, must designate special delegates, as required from time to time, from among the members of the legislature.

    SEC 62. - Permanent delegates

      SUBSEC (1)

        A person nominated as a permanent delegate must be eligible to be a member of the provincial legislature.

      SUBSEC (2)

        If a person who is a member of a provincial legislature is appointed as a permanent delegate, that person ceases to be a member of the legislature.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Permanent delegates are appointed for a term that expires immediately before the first sitting of the provincial legislature after its next election.

      SUBSEC (4)

        ITEMS
          A person ceases to be a permanent delegate if that person -

          ITEM (a)
            ceases to to be eligible to be a member of the provincial legislature for any reason other than being appointed as a permanent delegate;

          ITEM (b)
            becomes a member of the Cabinet;

          ITEM (c)
            has lost the confidence of the provincial legislature and is recalled by the party that nominated that person;

          ITEM (d)
            ceases to be a member of the party that nominated that person and is recalled by that party; or

          ITEM (e)
            is absent from the National council of Provinces without permission in circumstances for which the rules and orders of the Council prescribe loss of office as a permanent delegate.

      SUBSEC (5)

        Vacancies among the permanent delegates must be filled in terms of national legislation:

      SUBSEC (6)

        Before permanent delegates begin to perform their functions in the National Council of Provinces, they must swear or affirm faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

    SEC 63. - Sittings of National Council

      SUBSEC (1)

        The National Council of Provinces may determine the time and duration of its fittings and its recess periods.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The President may summon the National Council of Provinces to an extraordinary sitting at any time to conduct special business.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Sittings of the National Council of Provinces are permitted at places other than the seat of Parliament only on the grounds of public interest, security or convenience, and if provided for in the rules and orders of the Council.

    SEC 64. - Chairperson and Deputy Chairpersons

      SUBSEC (1)

        The National Council of Provinces must elect a Chairperson and two Deputy Chairpersons from among the delegates.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The Chairperson and one of the Deputy Chairpersons are elected from among the permanent delegates for five years unless their terms as delegates expire earlier.

      SUBSEC (3)

        The other Deputy Chairperson is elected for a term of one year, and must be succeeded by a delegate from another province, so that every province is represented in turn.

      SUBSEC (4)

        The President of the Constitutional Court must preside over the election of the Chairperson, or designate another judge to do so. The Chairperson presides over the election of the Deputy Chairpersons.

      SUBSEC (5)

        The procedure set out in Part A of {{>#att_4 Schedule 3}} applies to the may elect from among the delegates other presiding officers to assist the Chairperson and Deputy Chairpersons.

    SEC 65. - Decisions

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Except where the Constitution provides otherwise -

          ITEM (a)
            each province has one vote, which is cast on behalf of the province by the head of its delegation; and: -

          ITEM (b)
            all questions before the National Council of Provinces are agreed when at least five provinces vote in favour of the question.

      SUBSEC (2)

        An Act of Parliament, enacted in accordance with the procedure established by either subsection (1) or subsection (2) of {{>#sec_76 section 76}}, must provide for a uniform procedure in terms of which provincial legislatures confer authority on their delegations to cast votes on their behalf.

    SEC 66. - Participation by members of national executive

      SUBSEC (1)

        Cabinet members and Deputy Ministers may attend, and may speak in, the National Council of Provinces, but may not vote.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The National Council of Provinces may require a Cabinet member, a Deputy Minister or an official in the national executive or a provincial executive to attend a meeting of the Council or a committee of the Council.

    SEC 67. - Participation by local government representatives

      Not more than ten part-time representatives designated by organised local government in terms of {{>#sec_163 section 163}}, to represent the different categories of municipalities, may participate when necessary in the proceedings of the National Council of Provinces, but may not vote.

    SEC 68. - Powers of National Council

      ITEMS
        In exercising its legislative power; the National Council of Provinces may-

        ITEM (a)
          consider, pass, amend, propose amendments to or reject any legislation before the Council, in accordance with this Chapter; and

        ITEM (b)
          initiate or prepare legislation falling within a functional area listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}} or other legislation referred to in {{>#sec_76 section 76}}(3), but may not initiate or prepare money Bills.

    SEC 69. - Evidence or information before National Council

      ITEMS
        The National Council of Provinces or any of its committees may -

        ITEM (a)
          summon any person to appear before it to give evidence on oath or affirmation or to produce documents;

        ITEM (b)
          require any institution or person to report it;

        ITEM (c)
          compel, in terms of national legislation or the rules and orders, any person or institution to comply with a summons or requirement in terms of paragraph (a) or (b); and

        ITEM (d)
          receive petitions, representations or submissions from any interested persons or institutions.

    SEC 70. - Internal arrangements, proceedings and procedures of National Council

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The National Council of Provinces may -

          ITEM (a)
            determine and control its internal arrangements, proceedings and procedures; and

          ITEM (b)
            make rules and orders concerning its business, with due regard to representative and participatory democracy, accountability, transparency and public involvement.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The rules and orders of the National Council of Provinces must provide for -

          ITEM (a)
            the establishment, composition, powers, functions, procedures and duration of its committees;

          ITEM (b)
            the participation of all the provinces in its proceedings in a manner consistent with democracy; and

          ITEM (c)
            the participation in the proceedings of the Council and its committees of minority parties represented in the Council, in a manner consistent with democracy, whenever a matter is to be decided in accordance with {{>#sec_75 section 75}}.

    SEC 71. - Privilege

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Delegates to the National Council of Provinces and the persons referred to in sections {{>#sec_66 66}} and {{>#sec_67 67}} -

          ITEM (a)
            have freedom of speech in the Council and in its committees, subject to its rules and orders; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              are not liable to civil or criminal proceedings, arrest, imprisonment or damages for -

              ITEM (i)
                anything that they have said in, produced before or submitted to the Council or any of its committees; or

              ITEM (ii)
                anything revealed as a result of anything that they have said in, produced before or submitted to the Council or any of its committees.

      SUBSEC (2)

        Other privileges and immunities of the National Council of Provinces, delegates to the Council and persons referred to in sections {{>#sec_66 66}} and {{>#sec_67 67}} may be prescribed by national legislation.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Salaries, allowances and benefits payable to permanent members of the National Council of Provinces are a direct charge against the National Revenue Fund.

    SEC 72. - Public access to and involvement in National Council

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The National Council of Provinces must -

          ITEM (a)
            facilitate public involvement in the legislative and other processes of the Council and its committees; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              conduct its business in an open manner, and hold its sittings, and those of its committees, in oublic, but reasonable measures may be taken -

              ITEM (i)
                to regulate public access, including access of the media, to the Council and its committees; and

              ITEM (ii)
                to provide for the searching of any person and, where appropriate, the refusal of entry to, or the removal of, any person.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The National Council of Provinces may not exclude the public, including the media, from a sitting of a committee unless it is reasonable and justifiable to do so in an open and democratic society.

  SUBPART - National legislative process

    SEC 73. - Bills

      SUBSEC (1)

        Any Bill may be introduced in the National Assembly.

      SUBSEC (2)

        Only a Cabinet member or a Deputy Minister or a member or committee of the National Assembly, may introduce a Bill in the Assembly; but only the Cabinet member responsible for national financial matters may introduce a money Bill in the Assembly.

      SUBSEC (3)

        A Bill referred to in {{>#sec_76 section 76}}(3), except a money Bill, may be introduced in the National Council of Provinces.

      SUBSEC (4)

        Only a member or committee of the National Council of Provinces may introduce a Bill in the Council.

      SUBSEC (5)

        A Bill passed by the National Assembly must be referred to the National Council of Provinces if it must be considered by the Council. A Bill passed by the Council must be referred to the Assembly.

    SEC 74. - Bills amending the Constitution

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          {{>#sec_1 Section 1}} and this subsection may be amended by a Bill passed by -

          ITEM (a)
            the National Assembly, with a supporting vote of at least 75 per cent of its members; and

          ITEM (b)
            the National Council of Provinces, with a supporting vote of at least six provinces.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          {{>#chp_2 Chapter 2}} may be amended by a Bill passed by -

          ITEM (a)
            the National Assembly, with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members; and

          ITEM (b)
            the National Council of Provinces, with a supporting vote of at least six provinces.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          Any other provision of the Constitution may be amended by a Bill passed

          ITEM (a)
            by the National Assembly, with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              also by the National Council of Provinces, with a supporting vote of at least six provinces, if the amendment -

              ITEM (i)
                relates to a matter that affects the Council;

              ITEM (ii)
                alters provincial boundaries, powers, functions or institutions; or

              ITEM (iii)
                amends a provision that deals specifically with a provincial matter.

      SUBSEC (4)

        A Bill amending the Constitution may not include provisions other than constitutional amendments and matters connected with the amendments.

      SUBSEC (5)

        ITEMS
          At least 30 days before a Bill amending the Constitution is introduced in terms of {{>#sec_73 section 73}}(2), the person or committee intending to introduce the Bill must -

          ITEM (a)
            publish in the national //Government Gazette//, and in accordance with the rules and orders of the National Assembly, particulars of the proposed amendment for public comment;

          ITEM (b)
            submit, in accordance with the rules and orders of the Assembly, those particulars to the provincial legislatures for their views; and

          ITEM (c)
            submit, in accordance with the rules and orders of the National Council of Provinces, those particulars to the Council for a public debate, if the proposed amendment is not an amendment that is required to be passed by the Council.

      SUBSEC (6)

        ITEMS
          When a Bill amending the Constitution is introduced, the person or committee introducing the Bill must submit any written comments received from the public and the provincial legislatures —

          ITEM (a)
            to the Speaker for tabling in the National Assembly; and

          ITEM (b)
            in respect of amendments referred to in subsection (1), (2) or (3)(b), to the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces for tabling in the Council.

      SUBSEC (7)

        ITEMS
          A Bill amending the Constitution may not be put to the vote in the National Assembly within 30 days of -

          ITEM (a)
            its introduction, if the Assembly is sitting when the Bill is introduced; or

          ITEM (b)
            its tabling in the Assembly, if the Assembly is in recess when the Bill is introduced.

      SUBSEC (8)

        If a Bill referred to in subsection (3)(b), or any part of the Bill, concerns only a specific province or provinces, the National Council of Provinces may not pass the Bill or the relevant part unless it has been approved by the legislature or legislatures of the province or provinces concerned.

      SUBSEC (9)

        A Bill amending the Constitution has been passed by the National Assembly and, where applicable, by the National Council of Provinces, must be referred to the President for assent.

    SEC 75. - Ordinary Bills not affecting provinces

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          When the National Assembly passes a Bill other than a Bill to which the procedure set out in section {{>#sec_74 74}} or {{>#sec_76 76}} applies, the Bill must be referred to the National Council of Provinces and dealt with in accordance with the following procedure:

          ITEM (a)
            ITEMS
              The Council must -

              ITEM (i)
                pass the Bill;

              ITEM (ii)
                pass the Bill subject to amendments proposed by it; or

              ITEM (iii)
                reject the Bill.

          ITEM (b)
            If the Council passes the Bill without proposing amendments, the Bill must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (c)
            ITEMS
              If the Council rejects the Bill or passes it subject to amendments, the Assembly must reconsider the Bill, taking into account any amendment proposed by the Council, and may -

              ITEM (i)
                pass the Bill again, either, with or without amendments; or

              ITEM (ii)
                decide not to proceed with the Bill.

          ITEM (d)
            A Bill passed by the Assembly in terms of paragraph (c) must be submitted to the President for assent.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          When the National Council of Provinces votes on a question in terms of this section, {{>#sec_65 section 65}} does not apply; instead -

          ITEM (a)
            each delegate in a provincial delegation has one vote;

          ITEM (b)
            at least one third of the delegates must be present before a vote may be taken on the question; and

          ITEM (c)
            the question is decided by a majority of the votes cast, but if there is an equal number of votes on each side of the question, the delegate presiding must cast a deciding vote.

    SEC 76. - Ordinary Bills affecting provinces

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          When the National Assembly passes a Bill referred to in subsection (3), (4) or (5), the Bill must be referred to the National Council of Provinces and dealt with in accordance with the following procedure:

          ITEM (a)
            ITEMS
              The Council must -

              ITEM (i)
                pass the Bill;

              ITEM (ii)
                pass an amended Bill; or

              ITEM (iii)
                reject the Bill.

          ITEM (b)
            If the Council passes the Bill without amendment, the Bill must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (c)
            If the Council passes an amended Bill, the amended Bill must be referred to the Assembly, and if the Assembly passes the amended Bill, it must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (d)
            ITEMS
              If the Council rejects the Bill, or if the Assembly refuses to pass the Bill referred to it in terms of paragraph (c), the Bill and, where applicable, also the amended Bill, must be referred to the Mediation Committee, which may agree on -

              ITEM (i)
                the Bill as passed by the Assembly;

              ITEM (ii)
                the amended Bill as passed by the Council; or

              ITEM (iii)
                another version of the Bill.

          ITEM (e)
            If the Mediation Committee is unable to agree within 30 days of the Bill’s referral to it, the Bill lapses unless the Assembly again passes the Bill, but with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members.

          ITEM (f)
            If the Mediation Committee agrees on the Bill as passed by the Assembly, the Bill must be referred to the Council, and if the Council passes the Bill, the Bill must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (g)
            If the Mediation Committee agrees on the amended Bill as passed by the Council, the Bill must be referred to the Assembly, and if it is passed by the Assembly, it must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (h)
            If the Mediation Committee agrees on another version of the Bill, that version of the Bill must be referred to both the Assembly and the Council, and if it is passed by the Assembly and the Council, it must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (i)
            If a Bill referred to the Council in terms of paragraph (f) or (h) is not passed by the Council, the Bill lapses unless the Assembly passes the Bill with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members.

          ITEM (j)
            If a Bill referred to the Assembly in terms of paragraph (g) or (h) is not passed by the Assembly, that Bill lapses, but the Bill as originally passed by the Assembly may again be passed by the Assembly, but with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members.

          ITEM (k)
            A Bill passed by the Assembly in terms of paragraph (e), (i) or (j) must be submitted to the President for assent.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          When the National Council of Provinces passes a Bill referred to in subsection (3), the Bill must be referred to the National Assembly and dealt with in accordance with the following procedure:

          ITEM (a)
            ITEMS
              The Assembly must -

              ITEM (i)
                pass the Bill;

              ITEM (ii)
                pass an amended Bill; or

              ITEM (iii)
                reject the Bill.

          ITEM (b)
            A Bill passed by the Assembly in terms of paragraph (a)(i) must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (c)
            If the Assembly passes an amended Bill, the amended Bill must be referred to the Council, and if the Council passes the amended Bill, it must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (d)
            ITEMS
              If the Assembly rejects the Bill, or if the Council refuses to pass an amended Bill referred to it in terms of paragraph (c), the Bill and, where applicable, also the amended Bill must be referred to the Mediation Committee, which may agree on -

              ITEM (i)
                the Bill as passed by the Council;

              ITEM (ii)
                the amended Bill as passed by the Assembly; or

              ITEM (iii)
                another version of the Bill.

          ITEM (e)
            If the Mediation Committee is unable to agree within 30 days of the Bill’s referral to it, the Bill lapses.

          ITEM (f)
            If the Mediation Committee agrees on the Bill as passed by the Council, the Bill must be referred to the Assembly, and if the Assembly passes the Bill, the Bill must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (g)
            If the Mediation Committee agrees on the amended Bill as passed by the Assembly, the Bill must be referred to the Council, and if it is passed by the Council, it must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (h)
            If the Mediation Committee agrees on another version of the Bill, that version of the Bill must be referred to both the Council and the Assembly, and if it is passed by the Council and the Assembly, it must be submitted to the President for assent.

          ITEM (i)
            If a Bill referred to the Assembly in terms of paragraph (f) or (h) is not passed by the Assembly, the Bill lapses.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          A Bill must be dealt with in accordance with the procedure established by either subsection (1) or subsection (2) if it falls within a functional area listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}} or provides for legislation envisaged in any of the following sections:

          ITEM (a)
            {{>#sec_65 Section 65}}(2);

          ITEM (b)
            {{>#sec_163 section 163}};

          ITEM (c)
            {{>#sec_182 section 182}};

          ITEM (d)
            {{>#sec_195 section 195}}(3) and (4);

          ITEM (e)
            {{>#sec_196 section 196}}; and

          ITEM (f)
            {{>#sec_197 section 197}}.

      SUBSEC (4)

        ITEMS
          A Bill must be dealt with in accordance with the procedure established by subsection (1) if it provides for legislation -

          ITEM (a)
            envisaged in {{>#sec_44 section 44}}(2) or {{>#sec_220 220(}}(3); or

          ITEM (b)
            envisaged in {{>#chp_13 Chapter 13}}, and which affects the financial interests of the provincial sphere of government.

      SUBSEC (5)

        ITEMS
          A Bill envisaged in {{>#sec_42 section 42}}(6) must be dealt with in accordance with the procedure established by subsection (1), except that -

          ITEM (a)
            when the National Assembly votes on the Bill, the provisions of {{>#sec_53 section 53}}(1) do not apply; instead, the Bill may be passed only if a majority of the members of the Assembly vote in favour of it; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              if the Bill is referred to the Mediation Committee, the following rules apply:

              ITEM (i)
                If the National Assembly considers a Bill envisaged in subsection (1) (g) or (h), that Bill may be passed only if a majority of the members of the Assembly vote in favour ot it.

              ITEM (ii)
                If the National Assembly considers or reconsiders a Bill envisaged in subsection (1)(e), (i) or (j), that Bill may be passed only if at least two thirds of the members of the Assembly vote in favour of it.

      SUBSEC (6)

        This section does not apply to money Bills.

    SEC 77. - Money Bills

      SUBSEC (1)

        A Bill that appropriates money or imposes taxes, levies or duties is a money Bill. A money Bill may not deal with any other matter except a subordinate matter incidental to the appropriation of money or the imposition of taxes, levies or duties.

      SUBSEC (2)

        All money Bills must be considered in accordance with the procedure established by {{>#sec_75 section 75}}. An Act of Parliament must provide for a procedure to amend money Bills before Parliament.

    SEC 78. - Mediation Committee

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The Mediation Committee consists of-

          ITEM (a)
            nine members of the National Assembly elected by the Assembly in accordance with a procedure that is prescribed by the rules and orders of the Assembly and results in the representation of parties in substantially the same proportion that the parties are represented in the Assembly; and

          ITEM (b)
            one delegate from each provincial delegation in the National Council of Provinces, designated by the delegation

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The Mediation Committee has agreed on a version of a Bill, or decided a question, when that version, or one side of the question, is supported by -

          ITEM (a)
            at least five of the representatives of the National Assembly; and

          ITEM (b)
            at least five of the representatives of the National Council of Provinces.

    SEC 79. - Assent to Bills

      SUBSEC (1)

        The President must either assent to and sign a Bill passed in terms of this Chapter or, if the President has reservations about the constitutionality of the Bill, refer it back to the National Assembly for reconsideration.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The joint rules and orders must provide for the procedure for the reconsideration of a Bill by the National Assembly and the participation of the National Council of Provinces in the process.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          The National Council of Provinces must participate in the reconsideration of a Bill that the President has referred back to the National Assembly if -

          ITEM (a)
            the President’s reservations about the constitutionality of the Bill relate to a procedural matter that involves the Council; or

          ITEM (b)
            {{>#sec_74 section 74}}(1), (2) or (3)(b) or {{>#sec_76 76}} was applicable in the passing of the Bill.

      SUBSEC (4)

        ITEMS
          If, after reconsideration, a Bill fully accommodates the President’s reservations, the President must assent to and sign the Bill; if not, the President must either -

          ITEM (a)
            assent to and sign the Bill; or

          ITEM (b)
            refer it to the Constitutional Court for a decision on its constitutionality.

      SUBSEC (5)

        If the Constitutional Court decides that the Bill is constitutional, the President must assent to and sign it.

    SEC 80. - Application by members of National Assembly to Constitutional Court

      SUBSEC (1)

        Members of the National Assembly may apply to the Constitutional Court for an order declaring that all or part of an Act of Parliament is unconstitutional.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          An application -

          ITEM (a)
            must be supported by at least one third of the members of the National Assembly; and

          ITEM (b)
            must be made within 30 days of the date on which the President assented to and signed the Act.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          The Constitutional Court may order that all or part of an Act that is the subject of an application in terms of subsection (1) has no force until the Court has decided the application if -

          ITEM (a)
            the interests of justice require this; and

          ITEM (b)
            the application has a reasonable prospect of success.

      SUBSEC (4)

        If an application is unsuccessful, and did not have a reasonable prospect of success, the Constitutional Court may order the applicants to pay costs.

    SEC 81. - Publication of Acts

      A Bill assented to and signed by the President becomes an Act of Parliament, must be published promptly, and takes effect when published or on a date determined in terms of the Act.

    SEC 82. - Safekeeping of Acts of Parliament

      The signed copy of an Act of Parliament is conclusive evidence of the provisions of that Act and, after publication, must be entrusted to the Constitutional Court for safekeeping.

CHAP 5 - The President and national executive

  SEC 83. - The President

    ITEMS
      The President -

      ITEM (a)
        is the Head of State and head of the national executive;

      ITEM (b)
        must uphold, defend and respect the Constitution as the supreme law of the Republic; and

      ITEM (c)
        promotes the unity of the nation and that which will advance the Republic.

  SEC 84. - Powers and functions of President

    SUBSEC (1)

      The President has the powers entrusted by the Constitution and legislation, including those necessary to perform the functions of Head of State and head of the national executive.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        The President is responsible for -

        ITEM (a)
          assenting to and signing Bills;

        ITEM (b)
          referring a Bill back to the National Assembly for reconsideration of the Bill’s constitutionality;

        ITEM (c)
          referring a Bill to the Constitutional Court for a decision on the Bill’s constitutionality;

        ITEM (d)
          summoning the National Assembly, the National Council of Provinces or Parliament to an extraordinary sitting to conduct special business;

        ITEM (e)
          making any appointments that the Constitution or legislation requires the President to make, other than as head of the national executive;

        ITEM (f)
          appointing commissions of inquiry;

        ITEM (g)
          calling a national referendum in terms of an Act of Parliament;

        ITEM (h)
          receiving and recognising foreign diplomatic and consular representatives;

        ITEM (i)
          appointing ambassadors, plenipotentiaries, and diplomatic and consular representatives;

        ITEM (j)
          pardoning or reprieving offenders and remitting any fines, penalties or forfeitures; and

        ITEM (k)
          conferring honours.

  SEC 85. - Executive authority of the Republic

    SUBSEC (1)

      The executive authority of the Republic is vested in the President.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        The President exercises the executive authority, together with the other members of the Cabinet, by -

        ITEM (a)
          implementing national legislation except where the Constitution or an Act of Parliament provides otherwise;

        ITEM (b)
          developing and implementing national policy;

        ITEM (c)
          co-ordinating the functions of state departments and administrators;

        ITEM (d)
          preparing and initiating legislation; and

        ITEM (e)
          performing any other executive function provided for in the Constitution or in national legislation.

  SEC 86. - Election of President

    SUBSEC (1)

      At its first sitting after its election, and whenever necessary to fill a vacancy, the National Assembly must elect a woman or a man from among its members to be the President.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The President of the Constitutional Court must preside over the election of the President, or designate another judge to do so. The procedure set out in Part A of {{>#att_4 Schedule 3}} applies to the election of the President.

    SUBSEC (3)

      An election to fill a vacancy in the office of President must be held at a time and on a date determined by the President of the Constitutional Court, but not more than 30 days after the vacancy occurs.

  SEC 87. - Assumption of office by President

    When elected President, a person ceases to be a member of the National Assembly and, within five days, must assume office by swearing or affirming faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

  SEC 88. - Term of office of President

    SUBSEC (1)

      The President's term of office begins on assuming office and ends upon a vacancy occurring or when the person next elected President assumes office.

    SUBSEC (2)

      No person may hold office as President for more than two terms, but when a person is elected to fill a vacancy in the office of President, the period between that election and the next election of a President is not regarded as a term.

  SEC 89. - Removal of President

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        The National Assembly, by a resolution adopted with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members, may remove the President from office only on the grounds of -

        ITEM (a)
          a serious violation of the Constitution or the law;

        ITEM (b)
          serious misconduct; or

        ITEM (c)
          inability to perform the functions of office.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Anyone who has been removed from the office of President in terms of subsection (1) (a) or (b) may not receive any benefits of that office, and may not serve in any public office.

  SEC 90. - Acting President

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        When the President is absent from the Republic or otherwise unable to fulfil the duties of President, or during a vacancy in the office of President, an office-bearer  in the order below acts as President:

        ITEM (a)
          The Deputy President.

        ITEM (b)
          A Minister designated by the President.

        ITEM (c)
          A Minister designated by the other members of the Cabinet.

        ITEM (d)
          The Speaker until the National Assembly designates one of its other members.

    SUBSEC (2)

      An Acting President has the responsibilities, powers and functions of the President.

    SUBSEC (3)

      Before assuming the responsibilities, powers and functions of the President, the Acting President must swear or affirm faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

    SUBSEC (4)

      A person who as Acting President has sworn or affirmed faithfulness to the Republic need not repeat the swearing or affirming procedure for any subsequent term as Acting President during the period ending when the person next elected President assumes office.

      [[subsection (4) added by section 1 of the {{>/akn/za/act/1997/constitution-first-amendment Constitution First Amendment Act, 1997}}]]

  SEC 91. - Cabinet

    SUBSEC (1)

      The Cabinet consists of the President, as head of the Cabinet, a Deputy President and Ministers.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The President appoints the Deputy President and Ministers, assigns their powers and functions, and may dismiss them.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        The President -

        ITEM (a)
          must select the Deputy President from among the members of the National Assembly;

        ITEM (b)
          may select any number of Ministers from among the members of the Assembly; and

        ITEM (c)
          may select no more than two Ministers from outside the Assembly.

    SUBSEC (4)

      The President must appoint a member of the Cabinet to be the leader of government business in the National Assembly.

    SUBSEC (5)

      The Deputy President must assist the President in the execution of the functions of government.

  SEC 92. - Accountability and responsibilities

    SUBSEC (1)

      The Deputy President and Ministers are responsible for the powers and functions of the executive assigned to them by the President.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Members of the Cabinet are accountable collectively and individually to Parliament for the exercise of their powers and the performance of their functions.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        Members of the Cabinet must -

        ITEM (a)
          act in accordance with the Constitution; and

        ITEM (b)
          provide Parliament with full and regular reports concerning matters under their control.

  SEC 93. - Deputy Ministers

    The President may appoint Deputy Ministers from among members of the National Assembly to assist the members of the Cabinet, and may dismiss them.

  SEC 94. - Continuation of Cabinet after elections

    When an election of the National Assembly is held, the Cabinet, the Deputy President, Ministers and any Deputy Ministers remain competent to function until the person elected President by the next Assembly assumes office.

  SEC 95. - Oath or affirmation

    Before the Deputy President, Ministers and any Deputy Ministers begin to perform their functions, they must swear or affirm faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

  SEC 96. - Conduct of Cabinet members and Deputy Ministers

    SUBSEC (1)

      Members of the Cabinet and Deputy Ministers must act in accordance with a code of ethics prescribed by national legislation.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Members of the Cabinet and Deputy Ministers may not -

        ITEM (a)
          undertake any other paid work;

        ITEM (b)
          act in any way that is inconsistent with their office, or expose themselves to any situation involving the risk of a conflict between their official responsibilities and private interests; or

        ITEM (c)
          use their position or any information entrusted to them, to enrich themselves or improperly benefit any other person.

  SEC 97. - Transfer of functions

    ITEMS
      The President by proclamation may transfer to a member of the Cabinet -

      ITEM (a)
        the administration of any legislation entrusted to another member; or

      ITEM (b)
        any power or function entrusted by legislation to another member.

  SEC 98. - Temporary assignment of functions

    The President may assign to a Cabinet member any power or function of another member who is absent from office or is unable to exercise that power or perform that function.

  SEC 99. - Assignment of functions

    ITEMS
      A Cabinet member may assign any power or function that is to be exercised or performed in terms of an Act of Parliament to a member of a provincial Executive Council or to a Municipal Council. An assignment-

      ITEM (a)
        must be in terms of an agreement between the relevant Cabinet member and the Executice Council member or Municipal Council;

      ITEM (b)
        must be consistent with the Act of Parliament in terms of which the relevant power or function is exercised or performed; and

      ITEM (c)
        takes effect upon proclamation by the President.

  SEC 100. - National supervision of provincial administration

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        When a province cannot or does not fulfil an executive obligation in terms of legislation or the Constitution, the national executive may intervene by taking any appropriate steps to ensure fulfilment of that obligation, including -

        ITEM (a)
          issuing a directive to the provincial executive, describing the extent of the failure to fulfil its obligations and stating any steps required to meet its obligations; and

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            assuming responsibility for the relevant obligation in that province to the extent necessary to -

            ITEM (i)
              maintain essential national standards or meet established minimum standards for the rendering of a service;

            ITEM (ii)
              maintain economic unity;

            ITEM (iii)
              maintain national security; or

            ITEM (iv)
              prevent that province from taking unreasonable action that is prejudicial to the interests of another province or to the country as a whole.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        If the national executive intervenes in a province in terms of subsection (1)(b)—

        ITEM (a)
          notice of the intervention must be tabled in the National Council of Provinces within 14 days of its first sitting after the intervention began;

        ITEM (b)
          the intervention must end unless it is approved by the Council within 30 days of its first sitting after the intervention began; and

        ITEM (c)
          the Council must review the intervention regularly and make any appropriate recommendations to the national executive.

    SUBSEC (3)

      National legislation may regulate the process established by this section.

  SEC 101. - Executive decisions

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        A decision by the President must be in writing if it -

        ITEM (a)
          is taken in terms of legislation; or

        ITEM (b)
          has legal consequences.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A written decision by the President must be countersigned by another Cabinet member if that decision concerns a function assigned to that other Cabinet member.

    SUBSEC (3)

      Proclamations, regulations and other instruments of subordinate legislation must be accessible to the public.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        National legislation may specify the  manner in which, and the extent to which, instruments mentioned in subsection (3) must be -

        ITEM (a)
          tabled in Parliament; and

        ITEM (b)
          approved by Parliament.

  SEC 102. - Motions of no confidence

    SUBSEC (1)

      If the National Assembly, by a vote supported by a majority of its members, passes a motion of no confidence in the Cabinet excluding the President, the President must reconstitute the Cabinet.

    SUBSEC (2)

      If the National Assembly, by a vote supported by a majority of its members, passes a motion of no confidence in the President, the President and the other members of the Cabinet and any Deputy Ministers must resign.

  SUBPART - Provincial legislatures

    SEC 104. - Legislative authority of provinces

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The legislative authority of a province is vested in its provincial legislature, and confers on the provincial legislature the power -

          ITEM (a)
            to pass a constitution for its province or to amend any constitution passed by it in terms of sections {{>#sec_142 142}} and {{>#sec_143 143}};

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              to pass legislation for its province with regard to -

              ITEM (i)
                any matter within a functional area listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}};

              ITEM (ii)
                any matter within a functional area listed in {{>#att_6 Schedule 5}};

              ITEM (iii)
                any matter outside those functional areas, and that is expressly assigned to the province by national legislation; and

              ITEM (iv)
                any matter for which a provision of the Constitution envisages the enactment of provincial legislation; and

          ITEM (c)
            to assign any of its legislative powers to a Municipal Council in that province.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The legislature of a province, by a resolution adopted with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members, may request Parliament to change the name of that province.

      SUBSEC (3)

        A provincial legislature is bound only by the Constitution and, if it has passed a constitution for its province, also by that constitution, and must act in accordance with, and within the limits of the Constitution and that provincial constitution.

      SUBSEC (4)

        Provincial legislation with regard to a matter that is reasonably necessary for, or incidental to, the effective exercise of a power concerning any matter listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}}, is for all purposes legislation with regard to a matter listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}}.

      SUBSEC (5)

        A provincial legislature may recommend to the National Assembly legislation concerning any matter outside the authority of that legislature, or in respect of which an Act of Parliament prevails over a provincial law.

    SEC 105. - Composition and election of provincial legislatures

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          A provincial legislature consists of women and men elected as members in terms of an electoral system that -

          ITEM (a)
            is prescribed by national legislation;

          ITEM (b)
            is based on that province’s segment of the national common voters roll;

          ITEM (c)
            provides for a minimum voting age of 18 years; and

          ITEM (d)
            results, in general, in proportional representation.

      SUBSEC (2)

        A provincial legislature consists of between 30 and 80 members. The number of members, which may differ among the provinces, must be determined in terms of a formula prescribed by national legislation.

    SEC 106. - Membership

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Every citizen who is qualified to vote for the National Assembly is eligible to be a member of a provincial legislature, except -

          ITEM (a)
            ITEMS
              anyone who is appointed by, or is in the service of, the state and receives remuneration for that appointment or service, other than -

              ITEM (i)
                the Premier and other members of the Executive Council of a province; and

              ITEM (ii)
                other office-bearers whose functions are compatible with the functions of a member of a provincial legislature, and have been declared compatible with those functions by national legislation;

          ITEM (b)
            members of the National Assembly, permanent delegates to the National Council of Provinces or members of a Municipal Council;

          ITEM (c)
            unrehabilitated insolvents;

          ITEM (d)
            anyone declared to be of unsound mind by a court of the Republic; or

          ITEM (e)
            anyone who, after this section took effect, is convicted of an offence and sentenced to more than 12 months' imprisonment without the option of a fine, either in the Republic, or outside the Republic if the conduct constituting the offence would have been an offence in the Republic, but no one may be regarded as having been sentenced until an appeal against the conviction or sentence has been determined, or until the time for an appeal has expired. A disqualification under this paragraph ends five years after the sentence has been completed.

      SUBSEC (2)

        A person who is not eligible to be a member of a provincial legislature in terms of subsection (1) (a) or (b) may be a candidate for the legislature, subject to any limits or conditions established by national legislation.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          A person loses membership of a provincial legislature if that person—

          ITEM (a)
            ceases to be eligible; or

          ITEM (b)
            is absent from the legislature without permission in circumstances for which the rules and orders of the legislature prescribe loss of membership.

      SUBSEC (4)

        Vacancies in a provincial legislature must be filled in terms of national legislation.

    SEC 107. - Oath or affirmation

      Before members of a provincial legislature begin to perform their functions in the legislature, they must swear or affirm faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

    SEC 108. - Duration of provincial legislatures

      SUBSEC (1)

        A provincial legislature is elected for a term of five years.

      SUBSEC (2)

        If a provincial legislature is dissolved in terms of {{>#sec_109 section 109}}, or when its term expires, the Premier of the province, by proclamation, must call and set dates for an election, which must be held within 90 days of the date the legislature was dissolved or its term expired.

      SUBSEC (3)

        If the result of an election of a provincial legislature is not declared within the period referred to in {{>#sec_190 section 190}}, or if an election is set aside by a court, the President, by proclamation, must call and set dates for another election, which must be held within 90 days of the expiry of that period or of the date on which the election was setaside.

      SUBSEC (4)

        A provincial legislature remains competent to function from the time it is dissolved or its term expires, until the day before the first day of polling for the next legislature.

    SEC 109. - Dissolution of provincial legislatures before expiry of term

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The Premier of a province must dissolve the provincial legislature if -

          ITEM (a)
            the legislature has adopted a resolution to dissolve with a supporting vote of a majority of its members; and

          ITEM (b)
            three years have passed since the legislature was elected.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          An Acting Premier must dissolve the provincial legislature if -

          ITEM (a)
            there is a vacancy in the office of Premier; and

          ITEM (b)
            the legislature fails to elect a new Premier within 30 days after the vacancy occurred.

    SEC 110. - Sittings and recess periods

      SUBSEC (1)

        After an election, the first sitting of a provincial legislature must take place at a time and on a date determined by a judge designated by the President of the Constitutional Court, but not more than 14 days after the election result has been declared. A provincial legislature may determine the time and duration of its other sittings and its recess periods.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The Premier of a province may summon the provincial legislature to an extraordinary sitting at any time to conduct special business.

      SUBSEC (3)

        A provincial legislature may determine where it ordinarily will sit.

    SEC 111. - Speakers and Deputy Speakers

      SUBSEC (1)

        At the first sitting after its election, or when necessary to fill a vacancy, a provincial legislature must elect a Speaker and a Deputy Speaker from among its members.

      SUBSEC (2)

        A judge designated by the President of the Constitutional Court must preside over the election of a Speaker. The Speaker presides over the election of a Deputy Speaker.

      SUBSEC (3)

        The procedure set out in Part A of {{>#att_4 Schedule 3}} applies to the election of Speakers and Deputy Speakers.

      SUBSEC (4)

        A provincial legislature may remove its Speaker or Deputy Speaker from office by resolution. A majority of the members of the legislature must be present when the resolution is adopted.

      SUBSEC (5)

        In terms of its rules and orders, a provincial legislature may elect from among its members other presiding officers to assist the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker.

    SEC 112. - Decisions

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Except where the Constitution provides otherwise -

          ITEM (a)
            a majority of the members of a provincial legislature must be present before a vote may be taken on a Bill or an amendment to a Bill;

          ITEM (b)
            at least one third of the members must be present before a vote may be taken on any other question before the legislature; and

          ITEM (c)
            all questions before a provincial legislature are decided by a majority of the votes cast.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The member presiding at a meeting of a provincial legislature has no deliberative vote, but -

          ITEM (a)
            must cast a deciding vote when there is an equal number of votes on each side of a question; and

          ITEM (b)
            may cast a deliberative vote when a question must be decided witha supporting vote of at least two thirds of the members of the legislature.

    SEC 113. - Permanent delegates' rights in provincial legislatures

      A province's permanent delegates to the National Council of Provinces may attend, and may speak in, their provincial legislature and its committees, but may not vote. The legislature may require a permanent delegate to attend the legislature or its committees.

    SEC 114. - Powers of provincial legislatures

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          In exercising its legislative power, a provincial legislature may -

          ITEM (a)
            consider, pass, amend or reject any Bill before the legislature; and

          ITEM (b)
            initiate or prepare legislation, except money Bills.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          A provincial legislature must provide for mechanisms -

          ITEM (a)
            to ensure that all provincial executive organs of state in the province are accountable to it; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              to maintain oversight of -

              ITEM (i)
                the exercise of provincial executive authority in the province, including the implementation of legislation; and

              ITEM (ii)
                any provincial organ of state.

    SEC 115. - Evidence or information before provincial legislatures

      ITEMS
        A provincial legislature or any of its committees may -

        ITEM (a)
          summon any person to appear before it to give evidence on oath or affirmation, or to produce documents;

        ITEM (b)
          require any person or provincial institution to report to it;

        ITEM (c)
          compel, in terms of provincial legislation or the rules and orders, any person or institution to comply with a summons or requirement in terms of paragraph (a) or (b); and

        ITEM (d)
          receive petitions, representations or submissions from any interested persons or institutions.

    SEC 116. - Internal arrangements, proceedings and procedures of provincial legislatures

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          A provincial legislature may -

          ITEM (a)
            determine and control its internal arrangements, proceedings and procedures; and

          ITEM (b)
            make rules and orders concerning its business, with due regard to representative and participatory democracy, accountability, transparency and public involvement.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The rules and orders of a provincial legislature must provide for -

          ITEM (a)
            the establishment, composition, powers, functions, procedures and duration of its committees;

          ITEM (b)
            the participation in the proceedings of the legislature and its committees of minority parties represented in the legislature, in a manner consistent with democracy;

          ITEM (c)
            financial and administrative assistance to each party represented in the legislature, in proportion to its representation, to enable the party and its leader to perform their functions in the legislature effectively; and

          ITEM (d)
            the recognition of the leader of the largest opposition party in the legislature, as the Leader of the Opposition.

    SEC 117. - Privilege

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Members of a provincial legislature and the province’s permanent delegates to the National Council of Provinces -

          ITEM (a)
            have freedom of speech in the legislature and in its committees, subject to its rules and orders; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              are not liable to civil or criminal proceedings, arrest, imprisonment or damages for-

              ITEM (i)
                anything that they have said in, produced before or submitted to the legislature or any of its committees; or

              ITEM (ii)
                anything revealed as a result of anything that they have said in, produced before or submitted to the legislature or any of its committees.

      SUBSEC (2)

        Other privileges and immunities of a provincial legislature and its members may be prescribed by national legislation.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Salaries, allowances and benefits payable to members of a provincial legislature are a direct charge against the Provincial Revenue Fund.

    SEC 118. - Public access to and involvement in provincial legislatures

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          A provincial legislature must -

          ITEM (a)
            facilitate public involvement in the legislative and other processes of the legislature and its committees; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              conduct its business in an open manner, and hold its sittings, and those of its committees, in public, but reasonable measures may be taken —

              ITEM (i)
                to regulate public access, including access of the media, to the legislature and its committees; and

              ITEM (ii)
                to provide for the searching of any person and,  where appropriate, the refusal of entry to, or the removal of, any person.

      SUBSEC (2)

        A provincial legislature may not exclude the public, including the media, from a sitting of a committee unless it is reasonable and justifiable to do so in an open and democratic society.

    SEC 119. - Introduction of Bills

      Only members of the Executive Council of a province or a committee or member of a provincial legislature may introduce a Bill in the legislature; but only the member of the Executive Council who is responsible for financial matters in the province may introduce a money Bill in the legislature.

    SEC 120. - Money Bills

      SUBSEC (1)

        A Bill that appropriates money or imposes taxes, levies or duties is a money Bill. A money Bill may not deal with any other matter except a subordinate matter incidental to the appropriation of money or the imposition of taxes, levies or duties.

      SUBSEC (2)

        A provincial Act must provide for a procedure by which the province’s legislature may amend a money Bill.

    SEC 121. - Assent to Bills

      SUBSEC (1)

        The Premier of a province must either assent and sign a Bill passed by the provincial legislature in terms of this Chapter or if the Premier has reservations about the constitutionality of the Bill, refer it back to the legislature for reconsideration.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          If, after reconsideration, a Bill fully accommodates the Premier’s reservations, the Premier must assent to and sign the Bill; if not, the Premier must either -

          ITEM (a)
            assent to and sign the Bill; or

          ITEM (b)
            refer it to the Constitutional Court for a decision on its constitutionality.

      SUBSEC (3)

        If the Constitutional Court decides that the Bill is constitutional, the Premier must assent to and sign it.

    SEC 122. - Application by members to Constitutional Court

      SUBSEC (1)

        Members of a provincial legislature may apply to the Constitutional Court for an order declaring that all or part of a provincial Act is unconstitutional.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          An application -

          ITEM (a)
            must be supported by at least 20 per cent of the members of the legislature; and

          ITEM (b)
            must be made within 30 days of the date on which the Premier assented to and signed the Act.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          The Constitutional Court may order that all or part of an Act that is the subject of an application in terms of subsection (1) has no force until the Court has decided the application if -

          ITEM (a)
            the interests of justice require this; and

          ITEM (b)
            the application has a reasonable prospect of success.

      SUBSEC (4)

        If an application is unsuccessful, and did not have a reasonable prospect of success, the Constitutional Court may order the applicants to pay costs.

    SEC 123. - Publication of provincial Acts

      A Bill assented to and signed by the Premier of a province becomes a provincial Act, must be published promptly and takes effect when published or on a date determined in terms of the Act.

    SEC 124. - Safekeeping of provincial Acts

      The signed copy of a provincial Act is conclusive evidence of the provisions of that Act and, after publication, must be entrusted to the Constitutional Court for safekeeping.

  SUBPART - Provincial executives

    SEC 125. - Executive authority of provinces

      SUBSEC (1)

        The executive authority of a province is vested in the Premier of that province.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The Premier exercises the executive authority, together with the other members of the Executive Council, by -

          ITEM (a)
            implementing provincial legislation in the province;

          ITEM (b)
            implementing all national legislation within the functional areas listed in Schedule {{>#att_5 4}} and {{>#att_6 5}} except where the Constitution or an Act of Parliament provides otherwise;

          ITEM (c)
            administering in the province, national legislation outside the functional areas listed in Schedules {{>#att_5 4}} and {{>#att_6 5}}, the administration of which has been assigned to the provincial executive in terms of an Act of Parliament;

          ITEM (d)
            developing and implementing provincial policy;

          ITEM (e)
            co-ordinating the functions of the provincial administration and its departments;

          ITEM (f)
            preparing and initiating provincial legislation; and

          ITEM (g)
            performing any other function assigned to the provincial executive in terms of the Constitution or an Act of Parliament.

      SUBSEC (3)

        A province has executive authority in terms of subsection (2) (b) only to the extent that the province has the administrative capacity to assume effective responsibility. The national government by legislative and other measures, must assist provinces to develop the administrative capacity required for the effective exercise of their powers and performance of their functions referred to in subsection (2).

      SUBSEC (4)

        Any dispute concerning the administrative capacity of a province in regard to any function must be referred to the National Council of Provinces for resolution within 30 days of the date of the referral to the Council.

      SUBSEC (5)

        Subject to {{>#sec_100 section 100}}, the implementation of provincial legislation in a province is an exclusive provincial executive power.

      SUBSEC (6)

        ITEMS
          The provincial executive must act in accordance with -

          ITEM (a)
            the Constitution; and

          ITEM (b)
            the provincial constitution, if a constitution has been passed for the province.

    SEC 126. - Assignment of functions

      ITEMS
        A member of the Executive Council of a province may assign any power or function that is to be exercised or performed in terms of an Act of Parliament or a provincial Act, to a Municipal Council. An assignment -

        ITEM (a)
          must be in terms of an agreement between the relevant Executive Council member and the Municipal Council;

        ITEM (b)
          must be consistent with the Act in terms of which the relevant power or function is exercised or performed; and

        ITEM (c)
          takes effect upon proclamation by the Premier.

    SEC 127. - Powers and functions of Premiers

      SUBSEC (1)

        The Premier of a province has the powers and functions entrusted to that office by the Constitution and any legislation.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The Premier of a province is responsible for -

          ITEM (a)
            assenting to and signing Bills;

          ITEM (b)
            referring a Bill back to the provincial legislature for reconsideration of the Bill’s constitutionality;

          ITEM (c)
            referring a Bill to the Constitutional Court for a decision on the Bill’s constitutionality;

          ITEM (d)
            summoning the legislature to an extraordinary sitting to conduct special business;

          ITEM (e)
            appointing commissions of inquiry; and

          ITEM (f)
            calling a referendum in the province in accordance with national legislation.

    SEC 128. - Election of Premiers

      SUBSEC (1)

        At its first sitting after its election, and whenever necessary to fill a vacancy, a provincial legislature must elect a woman or a man from among its members to be the Premier of the province.

      SUBSEC (2)

        A judge designated by the President of the Constitutional Court must preside over the election of the Premier. The procedure set out in Part A of {{>#att_4 Schedule 3}} applies to the election of the Premier.

      SUBSEC (3)

        An election to fill a vacancy in the office of Premier must be held at a time and on a date determined by the President of the Constitutional Court, but not later than 30 days after the vacancy occurs.

    SEC 129. - Assumption of office by Premiers

      A Premier-elect must assume office within five days of being elected, by swearing or affirming faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

    SEC 130. - Term of office and removal of Premiers

      SUBSEC (1)

        A Premier’s term of office begins when the Premier assumes office and ends upon a vacancy occurring or when the person next elected Premier assumes office.

      SUBSEC (2)

        No person may hold office as Premier for more than two terms, but when a person is elected to fill a vacancy in the office of Premier, the period between that election and the next election of a Premier is not regarded as a term.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          The legislature of a province, by a resolution adopted with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members, may remove the Premier from office only on the grounds of -

          ITEM (a)
            a serious violation of the Constitution or the law;

          ITEM (b)
            serious misconduct; or

          ITEM (c)
            inability to perform the functions of office.

      SUBSEC (4)

        Anyone who has been removed from the office of Premier in terms of subsection (3) (a) or (b) may not receive any benefits of that office, and may not serve in any public office.

    SEC 131. - Acting Premiers

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          When the Premier is absent or otherwise unable to fulfil the duties of the office of Premier, or during a vacancy in the office of Premier, an office-bearer in the order below acts as the Premier:

          ITEM (a)
            A member of the Executive Council designated by the Premier.

          ITEM (b)
            A member of the Executive Council designated by the other members of the Council.

          ITEM (c)
            The Speaker, until the legislature designates one of its other members.

      SUBSEC (2)

        An Acting Premier has the responsibilities, powers and functions of the Premier.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Before assuming the responsibilities, powers and functions of the Premier, the Acting Premier must swear or affirm faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

    SEC 132. - Executive Councils

      SUBSEC (1)

        The Executive Council of a province consists of the Premier, as head of the Council, and no fewer than five and no more than ten members appointed by the Premier from among the members of the provincial legislature.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The Premier of a province appoints the members of the Executive Council, assigns their powers and functions, and may dismiss them.

    SEC 133. - Accountability and responsibilities

      SUBSEC (1)

        The members of the Executive Council of a province are responsible for the functions of the executive assigned to them by the Premier.

      SUBSEC (2)

        Members of the Executive Council of a province are accountable collectively and individually to the legislature for the exercise of their powers and the performance of their functions.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          Members of the Executive Council of a province must -

          ITEM (a)
            act in accordance with the Constitution and, if a provincial constitution has been passed for the province, also that constitution; and

          ITEM (b)
            provide the legislature with full and regular reports concerning matters under their control.

    SEC 134. - Continuation of Executive Councils after elections

      When an election of a provincial legislature is held, the Executive Council and its members remain competent to function until the person elected Premier by the next legislature assumes office.

    SEC 135. - Oath or affirmation

      Before members of the Executive Council of a province begin to perform their functions, they must swear or affirm faithfulness to the Republic and obedience to the Constitution, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}.

    SEC 136. - Conduct of members of Executive Councils

      SUBSEC (1)

        Members of the Executive Council of a province must act in accordance with a code of ethics prescribed by national legislation.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          Members of the Executive Council of a province may not -

          ITEM (a)
            undertake any other paid work;

          ITEM (b)
            act in any way that is inconsistent with their office, or expose themselves to any situation involving the risk of a conflict between their official responsibilities and private interests; or

          ITEM (c)
            use their position or any information entrusted to them, to enrich themselves or improperly benefit any other person.

    SEC 137. - Transfer of functions

      ITEMS
        The Premier by proclamation may transfer to a member of the Executive Council -

        ITEM (a)
          the administration of any legislation entrusted to another member; or

        ITEM (b)
          any power or function entrusted by legislation to another memeber.

    SEC 138. - Temporary assignment of functions

      The Premier of a province may assign to a member of the Executive Council any power or function of another member who is absent from office or is unable to exercise that power or perform that function.

    SEC 139. - Provincial supervision of local government

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          When a municipality cannot or does not fulfil an executive obligation in terms of legislation, the relevant provincial executive may intervene by taking any appropriate steps to ensure fulfilment of that obligation, including—

          ITEM (a)
            issuing a directive to the Municipal Council, describing the extent of the failure to fulfil its obligations and stating any steps required to meet its obligations; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              assuming responsibility for the relevant obligation in that municipality to the extent necessary -

              ITEM (i)
                to maintain essential national standards or meet established minimum standards for the rendering of a service;

              ITEM (ii)
                to prevent that Municipal Council from taking unreasonable action that is prejudicial to the interests of another municipality or to the province as a whole; or

              ITEM (iii)
                to maintain economic unity.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          If a provincial executive intervenes in a municipality in terms of subsection (1)(b) -

          ITEM (a)
            the intervention must end unless it is approved by the Cabinet member responsible for local government affairs within 14 days of the intervention;

          ITEM (b)
            notice of the intervention must be tabled in the provincial legislature and in the National Council of Provinces within 14 days of their respective first sittings after the intervention began;

          ITEM (c)
            the intervention must end unless it is approved by the Council within 30 days of its first sitting after the intervention began; and

          ITEM (d)
            the Council must review the intervention regularly and make any appropriate recommendations to the provincial executive.

      SUBSEC (3)

        National legislation may regulate the process established by this section.

    SEC 140. - Executive decisions

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          A decision by the Premier of a province must be in writing if it -

          ITEM (a)
            is taken in terms of legislation; or

          ITEM (b)
            has legal consequences.

      SUBSEC (2)

        A written decision by the Premier must be countersigned by another Executive Council member if that decision concerns a function assigned to that other member.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Proclamations, regulations and other instruments of subordinate legislation of a province must be accessible to the public.

      SUBSEC (4)

        ITEMS
          Provincial legislation may specify the manner in which, and the  extent to which, instruments mentioned in subsection (3) must be -

          ITEM (a)
            tabled in the provincial legislature; and

          ITEM (b)
            approved by the provincial legislature.

    SEC 141. - Motions of no confidence

      SUBSEC (1)

        If a provincial legislature, by a vote supported by a majority of its members, passes a motion of no confidence in the province’s Executive Council excluding the Premier, the Premier must reconstitute the Council.

      SUBSEC (2)

        If a provincial legislature, by a vote supported by a majority of its members, passes a motion of no confidence in the Premier, the Premier and the other members of the Executive Council must resign.

  SUBPART - Provincial constitutions

    SEC 142. - Adoption of provincial constitutions

      A provincial legislature may pass a constitution for the province or, where applicable, amend its constitution, if at least two thirds of its members vote in favour of the Bill.

    SEC 143. - Contents of provincial constitutions

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          A provincial constitution, or constitutional amendment, must not be inconsistent with this Constitution, but may provide for -

          ITEM (a)
            provincial legislative or executive structures and procedures that differ from those provided for in this Chapter; or

          ITEM (b)
            the institution, role, authority and status of a traditional monarch, where applicable.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          Provisions included in a provincial constitution or constitutional amendment in terms of paragraph (a) or (b) of subsection (1) -

          ITEM (a)
            must comply with the values in {{>#sec_1 section 1}} and with {{>#chp_3 Chapter 3}}; and

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              may not confer on the province any power or function that falls —

              ITEM (i)
                outside the area of provincial competence in terms of Schedule {{>#att_5 4}} and {{>#att_6 5}}; or

              ITEM (ii)
                outside the powers and functions conferred on the province by other sections of the Constitution.

    SEC 144. - Certification of provincial constitutions

      SUBSEC (1)

        If a provincial legislature has passed or amended a constitution, the Speaker of the legislature must submit the text of the constitution or constitutional amendment to the Constitutional Court for certification.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          No text of a provincial constitution or constitutional amendment becomes law until the Constitutional Court has certified -

          ITEM (a)
            that the text has been passed in accordance with {{>#sec_142 section 142}}; and

          ITEM (b)
            that the whole text complies with {{>#sec_143 section 143}}.

    SEC 145. - Signing, publication and safekeeping of provincial constitutions

      SUBSEC (1)

        The Premier of a province must assent to and sign the text of a provincial constitution or constitutional amendment that has been certified by the Constitutional Court.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The text assented to and signed by the Premier must be published in the national //Government Gazette// and takes effect on publication or on a later date determined in terms of that constitution or amendment.

      SUBSEC (3)

        The signed text of a provincial constitution or constitutional amendment is conclusive evidence of its provisions and, after publication, must be entrusted to the Constitutional Court for safekeeping.

  SUBPART - Conflicting laws

    SEC 146. - Conflicts between national and provincial legislation

      SUBSEC (1)

        This section applies to a conflict between national legislation and provincial legislation falling within a functional area listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}}.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          National legislation that applies uniformly with regard to the country as a whole prevails over provincial legislation if any of the following conditions is met:

          ITEM (a)
            The national legislation deals with a matter that cannot be regulated effectively by legislation enacted by the respective provinces individually.

          ITEM (b)
            ITEMS
              The national legislation deals with a matter that, to be dealt with effectively, requires uniformity across the nation, and the national legislation provides that uniformity by establishing -

              ITEM (i)
                norms and standards;

              ITEM (ii)
                frameworks; or

              ITEM (iii)
                national policies.

          ITEM (c)
            ITEMS
              The national legislation is necessary for-

              ITEM (i)
                the maintenance of national security;

              ITEM (ii)
                the maintenance of economic unity;

              ITEM (iii)
                the protection of the common market in respect of the mobility of goods, services, capital and labour;

              ITEM (iv)
                the promotion of economic activities across provincial boundaries;

              ITEM (v)
                the promotion of equal opportunity or equal access to government services; or

              ITEM (vi)
                the protection of the environment.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          National legislation prevails over provincial legislation if the national legislation is aimed at preventing unreasonable action by a province that -

          ITEM (a)
            is prejudicial to the economic, health or security interests of another province or the country as a whole; or

          ITEM (b)
            impedes the implementation of national economic policy.

      SUBSEC (4)

        When there is a dispute concerning whether national legislation is necessary for a purpose set out in subsection (2)(c) and that dispute comes before a court for resolution, the court must have due regard to the approval or the rejection of the legislation by the National Council of Provinces.

      SUBSEC (5)

        Provincial legislation prevails over national legislation if subsection (2) or (3) does not apply.

      SUBSEC (6)

        A law made in terms of an Act of Parliament or a provincial Act can prevail only if that law has been approved by the National Council of Provinces.

      SUBSEC (7)

        If the National Council of Provinces does not reach a decision within 30 days of its first sitting after a law was referred to it, that law must be considered for all purposes to have been approved by the Council.

      SUBSEC (8)

        If the National Council of Provinces does not approve a law referred to in subsection (6), it must, within 30 days of its decision, forward reasons for not approving the law to the authority that referred the law to it.

    SEC 147. - Other conflicts

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          If there is a conflict between national legislation and a provision of a provincial constitution with regard to -

          ITEM (a)
            a matter concerning which this Constitution  specifically requires or envisages the enactment of national legislation, the national legislation prevails over the affected provision of the provincial constitution;

          ITEM (b)
            national legislative intervention in terms of {{>#sec_44 section 44}}(2), the national legislation prevails over the provision of the provincial constitution; or

          ITEM (c)
            a matter within a functional area listed in {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}}, {{>#sec_146 section 146}} applies as if the affected provision of the provincial constitution were provincial legislation referred to in that section.

      SUBSEC (2)

        National legislation referred to in {{>#sec_44 section 44}}(2) prevails over provincial legislation in respect of matters within the functional areas listed in {{>#att_6 Schedule 5}}.

    SEC 148. - Conflicts that cannot be resolved

      If a dispute concerning a conflict cannot be resolved by a court, the national legislation prevails over the provincial legislation or provincial constitution.

    SEC 149. - States of legislation that does not prevail

      A decision by a court that legislation prevails over other legislation does not invalidate that other legislation, but that other legislation becomes inoperative for as long as the conflict remains.

    SEC 150. - Interpretation of conflicts

      When considering an apparent conflict between national and provincial legislation, or between national legislation and a provincial constitution, every court must prefer any reasonable interpretation of the legislation or constitution that avoids a conflict, over any alternative interpretation that results in a conflict.

CHAP 7 - Local government

  SEC 151. - Status of municipalities

    SUBSEC (1)

      The local sphere of government consists of municipalities, which must be established for the whole of the territory of the Republic.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The executive and legislative authority of a municipality is vested in its Municipal Council.

    SUBSEC (3)

      A municipality has the right to govern, on its own initiative, the local government affairs of its community, subject to national and provincial legislation, as provided for in the Constitution.

    SUBSEC (4)

      The national or a provincial government may not compromise or impede a municipality's ability or right to exercise its powers or perform its functions.

  SEC 152. - Objects of local government

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        The objects of local government are -

        ITEM (a)
          to provide democratic and accountable government for local communities;

        ITEM (b)
          to ensure the provision of services to communities in a sustainable manner;

        ITEM (c)
          to promote social and economic development;

        ITEM (d)
          to promote a safe and healthy environment; and

        ITEM (e)
          to encourage the involvement of communities and community organisations in the matters of local government.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A municipality must strive, within its financial and administrative capacity, to achieve the objects set out in subsection (1).

  SEC 153. - Developmental duties of municipalities

    ITEMS
      A municipality must -

      ITEM (a)
        structure and manage its administration and budgeting and planning processes to give priority to the basic needs of the community, and to promote the social and economic developmentof the community; and

      ITEM (b)
        participate in national and provincial development programmes.

  SEC 154. - Municipalities in co-operative government

    SUBSEC (1)

      The national government and provincial governments, by legislative and other measures, must support and strengthen the capacity of municipalities to manage their own affairs, to exercise their powers and to perform their functions.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Draft national or provincial legislation that affects the status, institutions, powers or functions of local government must be published for public comment before it is introduced in Parliament or a provincial legislature, in a manner that allows organised local government, municipalities and other interested persons an opportunity to make representations with regard to the draft legislation.

  SEC 155. - Establishment of municipalities

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        There are the following categories of municipality:

        ITEM (a)
          Category A: A municipality that has exclusive municipal executive and legislative authority in its area.

        ITEM (b)
          Category B: A municipality that shares municipal executive and legislative authority in its area with a category C municipality within whose area it falls.

        ITEM (c)
          Category C: A municipality that has municipal executive and legislative authority in an area that includes more than one municipality.

    SUBSEC (2)

      National legislation must define the different types of municipality that may be established within each category.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        National legislation must -

        ITEM (a)
          establish the criteria for determining when an area should have a single category A municipality or when it should have municipalities of both category B and category C;

        ITEM (b)
          establish criteria and procedures for the determination of municipal boundaries by an independent authority; and

        ITEM (c)
          subject to {{>#sec_229 section 229}}, make provision for an appropriate division of powers and functions between municipalities when an area has municipalities of both category B and category C. A division of powers and functions between a category B municipality and a category C municipality may differ from the division of powers and functions between another category B municipality and that category C municipality.

    SUBSEC (4)

      The legislation referred to in subsection (3) must take into account the need to provide municipal services in an equitable and sustainable manner.

    SUBSEC (5)

      Provincial legislation must determine the different types of municipality to be established in the province.

    SUBSEC (6)

      ITEMS
        Each provincial government must establish municipalities in its province in a manner consistent with the legislation enacted in terms of subsections (2) and (3) and, by legislative or other measures, must -

        ITEM (a)
          provide for the monitoring and support of local government in the province;

        ITEM (b)
          promote the development of local government capacity to enable municipalities to perform their functions and manage their own affairs.

    SUBSEC (6A)

      ITEMS
        If the criteria envisaged in subsection (3)(b) cannot be fulfilled without a municipal boundary extending across a provincial boundary—

        ITEM (a)
          ITEMS
            that municipal boundary may be determined across the provincial boundary, but only—

            ITEM (i)
              with the concurrence of the provinces concerned; and

            ITEM (ii)
              after the respective provincial executives have been authorised by national legislation to establish a municipality within that municipal area; and

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            national legislation may—

            ITEM (i)
              subject to subsection (5), provide for the establishment in that municipal area of a municipality of a type agreed to between the provinces concerned;

            ITEM (ii)
              provide a framework for the exercise of provincial executive authority in that municipal area and with regard to that municipality; and

            ITEM (iii)
              provide for the re-determination of municipal boundaries where one of the provinces concerned withdraws its support of a municipal boundary determined in terms of paragraph (a).

      [[subsection (6A) inserted by section 1 of the {{>/akn/za/act/1998/constitution-third-amendment Constitution Third Amendment Act, 1998}}]]

    SUBSEC (7)

      The national government, subject to {{>#sec_44 section 44}}, and the provincial governments have the legislative and executive authority to see to the effective performance by municipalities of their functions in respect of matters listed in Schedules 4 and 5, by regulating the exercise by municipalities of their executive authority referred to in {{>#sec_156 section 156}}(1).

  SEC 156. - Powers and functions of municipalities

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        A municipality has executive authority in respect of, and has the right to administer -

        ITEM (a)
          the local government matters listed in Part B of {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}} and Part B of {{>#att_6 Schedule 5}}; and

        ITEM (b)
          any other matter assigned to it by national or provincial legislation.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A municipality may make and administer by-laws for the effective administration of the matters which it has the right to administer.

    SUBSEC (3)

      Subject to {{>#sec_151 section 151}}(4), a by-law that conflicts with national or provincial legislation is invalid. If there is a conflict between a by-law and national or provincial legislation that is inoperative because of a conflict referred to in {{>#sec_149 section 149}}, the by-law must be regarded as valid for as long as that legislation is inoperative.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        The national government and provincial governments must assign to a municipality, by agreement and subject to any conditions, the administration of a matter listed in Part A of {{>#att_5 Schedule 4}} or Part A of {{>#att_6 Schedule 5}} which necessarily relates to local government, if-

        ITEM (a)
          that matter would most effectively be administered locally; and

        ITEM (b)
          the municipality has the capacity to administer it.

    SUBSEC (5)

      A municipality has the right to exercise any power concerning a matter reasonably necessary for, or incidental to, the effective performance of its functions.

  SEC 157. - Composition and election of Municipal Councils

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        A Municipal Council consists of -

        ITEM (a)
          members elected in accordance with subsections (2), (3), (4) and (5); or

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            if provided for by national legislation -

            ITEM (i)
              members appointed by other Municipal Councils to represent those other Councils; or

            ITEM (ii)
              both members elected in accordance with paragraph (a) and members appointed in accordance with subparagraph (i) of this paragraph.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        The election of members to a Municipal Council as anticipated in subsection (1)(a) must be in accordance with national legislation, which must prescribe a system -

        ITEM (a)
          of proportional representation based on that municipality segment of the national common voters roll, and which provides for the election of members from lists of party candidates drawn up in a party’s order of preference; or

        ITEM (b)
          of proportional representation as described in paragraph (a) combined with a system of ward representation based on that municipality’s segment of the national common voters roll.

    SUBSEC (3)

      An electoral system in terms of subsection (2) must ensure that the total number of members elected from each party reflects the total proportion of the votes recorded for those parties.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          If the electoral system includes ward representation, the delimitation of wards must be done by an independent authority appointed in terms of, and operating according to, procedures and criteria prescribed by national legislation.

        ITEM (b)
          Where a municipal boundary bas been determined in terms of {{>#sec_155 section 155}}(6A), a ward delimited within that municipal boundary may not extend across the provincial boundary concerned.

      [[subsection (4) substituted by section 2 of the {{>/akn/za/act/1998/constitution-third-amendment Constitution Third Amendment Act, 1998}}]]

    SUBSEC (5)

      A person may vote in a municipality only if that person is registered on that municipality’s segment of the national common voters roll.

    SUBSEC (6)

      The national legislation referred to in subsection (1)(b) must establish a system that allows for parties and interests reflected within the Municipal Council making the appointment, to be fairly represented in the Municipal Council to which the appointment is made.

  SEC 158. - Membership of Municipal Councils

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Every citizen who is qualified to vote for a Municipal Council is eligible to be a member of that Council, except -

        ITEM (a)
          anyone who is appointed by, or is in the service of, the municipality and receives remuneration for that appointment or service, and who has not been exempted from this disqualification in terms of national legislation;

        ITEM (b)
          anyone who is appointed by, or is in the service of, the state in another sphere, and receives remuneration for that appointment or service, and who has been disqualified from membership of a Municipal Council in terms of national legislation;

        ITEM (c)
          anyone who is disqualified from voting for the National Assembly or is disqualified in terms of {{>#sec_47 section 47}}(1)(c), (d) or (e) from being a member of the Assembly;

        ITEM (d)
          a member of the National Assembly, a delegate to the National Council of Provinces or a member of a provincial legislature; but this disqualification does not apply to a member of a Municipal Council representing local government in the National Council; or

        ITEM (e)
          a member of another Municipal Council; but this disqualification does not apply to a member of a Municipal Council representing that Council in another Municipal Council of a different category.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A person who is not eligible to be a member of a Municipal Council in terms of subsection (1)(a), (b), (d) or (e) may be a candidate for the Council, subject to any limits or conditions established by national legislation.

  SEC 159. - Terms of Municipal Councils

    SUBSEC (1)

      The term of a Municipal Council may be no more than five years, as determined by national legislation.

    SUBSEC (2)

      If a Municipal Council is dissolved in terms of national legislation, or when its term expires, an election must be held within 90 days of the date that Council was dissolved or its term expired.

    SUBSEC (3)

      A Municipal Council, other than a Council that has been dissolved following an intervention in terms of {{>#sec_139 section 139}}, remains competent to function from the time it is dissolved or its term expires, until the newly elected Council has been declared elected.

      [[section 159 substituted by section 1 of the {{>/akn/za/act/1998/constitution-second-amendment Constitution Second Amendment Act, 1998}}]]

  SEC 160. - Internal procedures

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        A Municipal Council -

        ITEM (a)
          makes decisions concerning the exercise of all the powers and the performance of all the functions of the municipality;

        ITEM (b)
          must elect its chairperson;

        ITEM (c)
          may elect an executive committee and other committees, subject to national legislation; and

        ITEM (d)
          may employ personnel that are necessary for the effective performance of its functions.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        The following functions may not be delegated by a Municipal Council:

        ITEM (a)
          The passing of by-laws;

        ITEM (b)
          the approval of budgets;

        ITEM (c)
          the imposition of rates and other taxes, levies and duties; and

        ITEM (d)
          the raising of loans.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          A majority of the members of a Municipal Council must be present before a vote may be taken on any matter.

        ITEM (b)
          All questions concerning matters mentioned in subsection (2) are determined by a decision taken by a Municipal Council with a supporting vote of a majority of its members.

        ITEM (c)
          All other questions before a Municipal Council are decided by a majority of the votes cast.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        No by-law may be passed by a Municipal Council unless -

        ITEM (a)
          all the members of the Council have been given reasonable notice; and

        ITEM (b)
          the proposed by-law has been published for public comment.

    SUBSEC (5)

      ITEMS
        National legislation may provide criteria for determining -

        ITEM (a)
          the size of a Municipal Council;

        ITEM (b)
          whether Municipal Councils may elect an executive committee or any other committee; or

        ITEM (c)
          the size of the executive committee or any other committee of a Municipal Council functions of its committees.

    SUBSEC (6)

      ITEMS
        A Municipal Council may make by-laws which prescribe rules and orders for -

        ITEM (a)
          its internal arrangements;

        ITEM (b)
          its business and proceedings; and

        ITEM (c)
          the establishment, composition, procedures, powers and functions of its committees.

    SUBSEC (7)

      A Municipal Council must conduct its business in an open manner and may close its sittings, or those of its committees, only when it is reasonable to do so having regard to the nature of the business being transacted.

    SUBSEC (8)

      ITEMS
        Members of a Municipal Council are entitled to participate in its proceedings and those of its committees in a manner that -

        ITEM (a)
          allows parties and interests reflected within the Council to be fairly represented;

        ITEM (b)
          is consistent with democracy; and

        ITEM (c)
          may be regulated by national legislation.

  SEC 161. - Privilege

    Provincial legislation within the framework of national legislation may provide for privileges and immunities of Municipal Councils and their members.

  SEC 162. - Publication of municipal by-laws

    SUBSEC (1)

      A municipal by-law may be enforced only after it has been published in the official gazette of the relevant province.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A provincial official gazette must publish a municipal by-law upon request by the municipality.

    SUBSEC (3)

      Municipal by-laws must be accessible to the public.

  SEC 163. - Organised local government

    ITEMS
      An Act of Parliament enacted in accordance with the procedure established by {{>#sec_76 section 76}} must -

      ITEM (a)
        provide for the recognition of national and provincial organisations representing municipalities; and

      ITEM (b)
        ITEMS
          determine procedures by which local government may—

          ITEM (i)
            consult with the national or a provincial government;

          ITEM (ii)
            designate representatives to participate in the National Council of Provinces; and

          ITEM (iii)
            nominate persons to the Financial and Fiscal Commission.

  SEC 164. - Other matters

    Any matter concerning local government not dealt with in the Constitution may be prescribed by national legislation or by provincial legislation within the framework of national legislation.

CHAP 8 - Courts and administration of justice

  SEC 165. - Judicial authority

    SUBSEC (1)

      The judicial authority of the Republic is vested in the courts.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The courts are independent and subject only to the Constitution and the law, which they must apply impartially and without fear, favour or prejudice.

    SUBSEC (3)

      No person or organ of state may interfere with the functioning of the courts.

    SUBSEC (4)

      Organs of state, through legislative and other measures, must assist and protect the courts to ensure the independence, impartiality, dignity, accessibility and effectiveness of the courts.

    SUBSEC (5)

      An order or decision issued by a court binds all persons to whom and organs of state to which it applies.

  SEC 166. - Judicial system

    ITEMS
      The courts are -

      ITEM (a)
        the Constitutional Court;

      ITEM (b)
        the Supreme Court of Appeal;

      ITEM (c)
        the High Courts, including any high court of appeal that may be established by an Act of Parliament to hear appeals from High Courts;

      ITEM (d)
        the Magistrates' Courts; and

      ITEM (e)
        any other court established or recognised in terms of an Act of Parliament, including any court of a status similar to either the High Courts or the Magistrates’ Courts.

  SEC 167. - Constitutional Court

    SUBSEC (1)

      The Constitutional Court consists of a President, a Deputy President and nine other judges.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A matter before the Constitutional Court must be heard by at least eight judges.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        The Constitutional Court-

        ITEM (a)
          is the highest court in all constitutional matters;

        ITEM (b)
          may decide only constitutional matters, and issues connected with decisions on constitutional matters; and

        ITEM (c)
          makes the final decision whether a matter is a constitutional matter or whether an issue is connected with a decision on a constitutional matter.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        Only the Constitutional Court may -

        ITEM (a)
          decide disputes between organs of state in the national or provincial sphere concerning the constitutional status, power or functions of any of those organs of state;

        ITEM (b)
          decide on the constitutionality of any parliamentary or provincial Bill, but may do so only in the circumstances anticipated in {{>#sec_79 section 79}} or {{>#sec_121 121}};

        ITEM (c)
          decide applications envisaged in {{>#sec_80 section 80}} or {{>#sec_122 122}};

        ITEM (d)
          decide on the constitutionality of any amendment to the Constitution;

        ITEM (e)
          decide that Parliament or the President has failed to fulfil a constitutional obligation; or

        ITEM (f)
          certify a provincial constitution in terms of {{>#sec_144 section 144}}.

    SUBSEC (5)

      The Constitutional Court makes the final decision whether an Act of Parliament, a provincial Act or conduct of the President is constitutional, and must confirm any order of invalidity made by the Supreme Court of Appeal, a High Court, or a court of similar status, before that order has any force.

    SUBSEC (6)

      ITEMS
        National legislation or the rules of the Constitutional Court must allow a person, when it is in the interests of justice and with leave of the Constitutional Court-

        ITEM (a)
          to bring a matter directly to the Constitutional Court; or

        ITEM (b)
          to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court from any other court.

    SUBSEC (7)

      A constitutional matter includes any issue involving the interpretation, protection or enforcement of the Constitution.

  SEC 168. - Supreme Court of Appeal

    SUBSEC (1)

      The Supreme Court of Appeal consists of a Chief Justice, a Deputy Chief Justice and the number of judges of appeal determined by an Act of Parliament.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A matter before the Supreme Court of Appeal must be decided by the number of judges determined by an Act of Parliament.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        The Supreme Court of Appeal may decide appeals in any matter. It is the highest court of appeal except in constitutional matters, and may decide only -

        ITEM (a)
          appeals;

        ITEM (b)
          issues connected with appeals; and

        ITEM (c)
          any other matter that may be referred to it in circumstances defined by an Act of Parliament.

  SEC 169. - High Courts

    ITEMS
      A High Court may decide -

      ITEM (a)
        ITEMS
          any constitutional matter except a matter that -

          ITEM (i)
            only the Constitutional Court may decide; or

          ITEM (ii)
            is assigned by an Act of Parliament to another court of a status similar to a High Court; and

      ITEM (b)
        any other matter not assigned to another court by an Act of Parliament.

  SEC 170. - Magistrates' Courts and other courts

    Magistrates' Courts and all other courts may decide any matter determined by an Act of Parliament, but a court of a status lower than a High Court may not enquire into or rule on the constitutionality of any legislation or any conduct of the President.

  SEC 171. - Court procedures

    All courts function in terms of national legislation, and their rules and procedures must be provided for in terms of national legislation.

  SEC 172. - Powers of courts in constitutional matters

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        When deciding a constitutional matter within its power; a court -

        ITEM (a)
          must declare that any law or conduct that is inconsistent with the Constitution  is invalid to the extent of its inconsistency; and

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            may make any order that is just and equitable, including -

            ITEM (i)
              an order limiting the retrospective effect of the declaration of invalidity; and

            ITEM (ii)
              an order suspending the declaration of invalidity for any period and on any conditions, to allow the competent authority to correct the defect.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          The Supreme Court of Appeal, a High Court or a court of similar status may make an order concerning the constitutional validity of an Act of Parliament, a provincial Act or any conduct of the President, but an order of constitutional invalidity has no force unless it is confirmed by the Constitutional Court.

        ITEM (b)
          A court which makes an order of constitutional invalidity may grant a temporary interdict or other temporary relief to a party, or may adjourn the proceedings, pending a decision of the Constitutional Court on the validity of that Act or conduct.

        ITEM (c)
          National legislation must provide for the referral of an order of constitutional invalidity to the Constitutional Court.

        ITEM (d)
          Any person or organ of state with a sufficient interest may appeal, or apply, directly to the Constitutional Court to confirm or vary an order of constitutional invalidity by a court in terms of this subsection.

  SEC 173. - Inherent power

    The Constitutional Court, Supreme Court of Appeal and High Courts have the inherent power to protect and regulate their own process, and to develop the common low, taking into account the interests of justice.

  SEC 174. - Appointment of judicial officers

    SUBSEC (1)

      Any appropriately qualified woman or man who is a fit and proper person may be appointed as a judicial officer. Any person to be appointed to the Constitutional Court must also be a South African citizen.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The need for the judiciary to reflect broadly the racial and gender composition of South Africa must be considered when judicial officers are appointed.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The President as head of the national executive, after consulting the Judicial Service Commission and the leaders of parties represented in the National Assembly, appoints the President and Deputy President of the Constitutional Court and, after consulting the Judicial Service Commission, appoints the Chief Justice and Deputy Chief Justice.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        The other judges of the Constitutional Court are appointed by the President, as head of the national executive, after consulting the President of the Constitutional Court and the leaders of parties represented in the National Assembly, in accordance with the following procedure.

        ITEM (a)
          The Judicial Service Commission must prepare a list of nominees with three names more than the number of appointments to be made, and submit the list to the President.

        ITEM (b)
          The President may make appointments from the list, and must advise the Judicial Service Commission, with reasons, if any of the nominees are unacceptable and any appointment remains to be made.

        ITEM (c)
          The Judicial Service Commission must supplement the list with further nominees and the President must make the remaining appointments from the supplemented list.

    SUBSEC (5)

      At all times, at least four members of the Constitutional Court must be persons who were judges at the time they were appointed to the Constitutional Court.

    SUBSEC (6)

      The President must appoint the judges of all other courts on the advice of the Judicial Service Commission.

    SUBSEC (7)

      Other judicial officers must be appointed in terms of an Act of Parliament which must ensure that the appointment, promotion, transfer or dismissal of, or disciplinary steps against, these judicial officers take place without favour or prejudice.

    SUBSEC (8)

      Before judicial officers begin to perform their functions, they must take an oath or affirm, in accordance with {{>#att_3 Schedule 2}}, that they will uphold and protect the Constitution.

  SEC 175. - Acting judges

    SUBSEC (1)

      The President may appoint a woman or a man to be an acting judge of the Constitutional Court if there is a vacancy or if a judge is absent. The appointment must be made on the recommendation of the Cabinet member responsible for the administration of justice acting with the concurrence of the President of the Constitutional Court and the Chief Justice.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The Cabinet member responsible for the administration of justice must appoint acting judges to other courts after consulting the senior judge of the court on which the acting judge will serve.

  SEC 176. - Terms of office and remuneration

    SUBSEC (1)

      A Constitutional Court judge is appointed for a non-renewable term of 12 years, but must retire at the age of 70.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Other judges hold office until they are discharged from active service in terms of an Act of Parliament.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The salaries, allowances and benefits of judges may not be reduced.

  SEC 177. - Removal

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        A judge may be removed from office only if-

        ITEM (a)
          the Judicial Service Commission finds that the judge suffers from an incapacity, is grossly incompetent or is guilty of gross misconduct; and

        ITEM (b)
          the National Assembly calls for that judge to be removed, by a resolution adopted with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The President must remove a judge from office upon adoption of a resolution calling for that judge to be removed.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The President, on the advice of the Judicial Service Commission, may suspend a judge who is the subject of a procedure in terms of subsection (1).

CHAP 10 - Public administration

  SEC 195. - Basic values and principles governing public administration

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Public administration must be governed by the democratic values and principles enshrined in the Constitution, including the following principles:

        ITEM (a)
          A high standard of professional ethics must be promoted and maintained.

        ITEM (b)
          Efficient, economic and effective use of resources must be promoted.

        ITEM (c)
          Public administration must be development-oriented.

        ITEM (d)
          Services must be provided impartially, fairly, equitably and without bias.

        ITEM (e)
          People’s needs must be responded to, and the public must be encouraged to participate in policy-making.

        ITEM (f)
          Public administration must be accountable.

        ITEM (g)
          Transparency must be fostered by providing the public with timely, accessible and accurate information.

        ITEM (h)
          Good human-resource management and career-development practices, to maximise human potential, must be cultivated.

        ITEM (i)
          Public administration must be broadly representative of the South African people, with employment and personnel management practices based on ability, objectivity, fairness, and the need to redress the imbalances of the past to achieve broad representation.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        The above principles apply to -

        ITEM (a)
          administration in every sphere of government;

        ITEM (b)
          organs of sate; and

        ITEM (c)
          public enterprises.

    SUBSEC (3)

      National legislation must ensure the promotion of the values and principles listed in subsection (1).

    SUBSEC (4)

      The appointment in public administration of a number of persons on policy considerations is not precluded, but national legislation must regulate these appointments in the public service.

    SUBSEC (5)

      Legislation regulating public administration may differentiate between different sectors, administrations or institutions.

    SUBSEC (6)

      The nature and functions of different sectors, administrations or institutions of public administration are relevant factors to be taken into account in legislation regulating public administration.

CHAP 11 - Security services

  SEC 198. - Governing principles

    ITEMS
      The following principles govern national security in the Republic:

      ITEM (a)
        National security must reflect the resolve of South Africans, as individuals and as a nation, to live as equals, to live in peace and harmony, to be free from fear and want and to seek a better life.

      ITEM (b)
        The resolve to live in peace and harmony precludes any South African citizen from participating in armed conflict, nationally or internationally, except as provided for in terms of the Constitution or national legislation.

      ITEM (c)
        National security must be pursued in compliance with the law, including international law.

      ITEM (d)
        National security is subject to the authority of Parliament and the national executive.

  SEC 199. - Establishment, structuring and conduct of security services

    SUBSEC (1)

      The security services of the Republic consist of a single defence force, a single police service and any intelligence services established in terms of the Constitution.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The defence force is the only lawful military force in the Republic.

    SUBSEC (3)

      Other than the security services established in terms of the Constitution, armed organisations or services may be established only in terms of national legislation.

    SUBSEC (4)

      The security services must be structured and regulated by national legislation.

    SUBSEC (5)

      The security services must act, and must teach and require their members to act, in accordance with the Constitution and the law, including customary international law and international agreements binding on the Republic.

    SUBSEC (6)

      No member of any security service may obey a manifestly illegal order.

    SUBSEC (7)

      ITEMS
        Neither the security services, nor any of their members, may, in the performance of their functions -

        ITEM (a)
          prejudice a political party interest that is legitimate in terms of the Constitution; or

        ITEM (b)
          further, in a partisan manner any interest of a political party.

    SUBSEC (8)

      To give effect to the principles of transparency and accountability, multi-party parliamentary committees must have oversight of all security services in a manner determined by national legislation or the rules and orders of Parliament.

  SUBPART - Defence

    SEC 200. - Defence force

      SUBSEC (1)

        The defence force must be structured and managed as a disciplined military force.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The primary object of the defence force is to defend and protect the Republic, its territorial integrity and its people in accordance with the Constitution and the principles of international law regulating the use of force.

    SEC 201. - Political responsibility

      SUBSEC (1)

        A member of the Cabinet must be responsible for defence.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          Only the President, as head of the national executive, may authorise the employment of the defence force -

          ITEM (a)
            in co-operation with the police service;

          ITEM (b)
            in defence of the Republic; or

          ITEM (c)
            in fulfilment of an international obligation.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          When the defence force is employed for any purpose mentioned in subsection (2), the President must inform Parliament, promptly and in appropriate detail, of -

          ITEM (a)
            the reasons for the employment of the defence force;

          ITEM (b)
            any place where the force is being employed;

          ITEM (c)
            the number of people involved; and

          ITEM (d)
            the period for which the force is expected to be employed.

      SUBSEC (4)

        If Parliament does not sit during the first seven days after the defence force is employed as envisaged in subsection (2), the President must provide the information required in subsection (3) to the appropriate oversight committee.

    SEC 202. - Command of defence force

      SUBSEC (1)

        The President as head of the national executive is Commander-in-chief of the defence force, and must appoint the Military Command of the defence force.

      SUBSEC (2)

        Command of the defence force must be exercised in accordance with the directions of the Cabinet member responsible for defence, under the authority of the President.

    SEC 203. - State of national defence

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The President as head of the national executive may declare a state of national defence, and must inform Parliament promptly and in appropriate detail of -

          ITEM (a)
            the reasons for the declaration;

          ITEM (b)
            any place where the defence force is being employed; and

          ITEM (c)
            the number of people involved.

      SUBSEC (2)

        If Parliament is not sitting when a state of national defence is declared, the President must summon Parliament to an extraordinary sitting within seven days of the declaration.

      SUBSEC (3)

        A declaration of a state of national defence lapses unless it is approved by Parliament within seven days of the declaration.

    SEC 204. - Defence civilian secretariat

      A civilian secretariat for defence must be established by national legislation to function under the direction of the Cabinet member responsible for defence.

  SUBPART - Police

    SEC 205. - Police service

      SUBSEC (1)

        The national police service must be structured to function in the national, provincial and, where appropriate, local spheres of government.

      SUBSEC (2)

        National legislation must establish the powers and functions of the police service and must enable the police service to discharge its responsibilities effectively, taking into account the requirements of the provinces.

      SUBSEC (3)

        The objects of the police service are to prevent, combat and investigate crime, to maintain public order, to protect and secure the inhabitants of the Republic and their property, and to uphold and enforce the law.

    SEC 206. - Political responsibility

      SUBSEC (1)

        A member of the Cabinet must be responsible for policing and must determine national policing policy after consulting the provincial governments and taking into account the policing needs and priorities of the provinces as determined by the provincial executives.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The national policing policy may make provision for different policies in respect of different provinces after taking into account the policing needs and priorities of these provinces.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          Each province is entitled -

          ITEM (a)
            to monitor police conduct;

          ITEM (b)
            to oversee the effectiveness and efficiency of the police service, including receiving reports on the police service;

          ITEM (c)
            to promote good relations between the police and the community;

          ITEM (d)
            to assess the effectiveness of visible policing; and

          ITEM (e)
            to liaise with the Cabinet member responsible for policing with respect to crime and policing in the province.

      SUBSEC (4)

        ITEMS
          A provincial executive is responsible for policing functions -

          ITEM (a)
            vested in it by this Chapter;

          ITEM (b)
            assigned to it in terms of national legislation; and

          ITEM (c)
            allocated to it in the national policing policy.

      SUBSEC (5)

        ITEMS
          In order to perform the functions set out in subsection (3), a province -

          ITEM (a)
            may investigate, or appoint a commission of inquiry into, any complaints of police inefficiency or a breakdown in relations between the police and any community; and

          ITEM (b)
            must make recommendations to the Cabinet member responsible for policing.

      SUBSEC (6)

        On receipt of a complaint lodged by a provincial executive, an independent police complaints body established by national legislation must investigate any alleged misconduct of, or offence committed by, a member of the police service in the province.

      SUBSEC (7)

        National legislation must provide a framework for the establishment, powers, functions and control of municipal police services.

      SUBSEC (8)

        A committee composed of the Cabinet member and the members of the Executive Councils responsible for policing must be established to ensure effective co-ordination of the police service and effective co-operation among the spheres of government.

      SUBSEC (9)

        A provincial legislature may require the provincial commissioner of the province to appear before it or any of its committees to answer questions.

    SEC 207. - Control of police service

      SUBSEC (1)

        The President as head of the national executive must appoint a woman or a man as the National Commissioner of the police service, to control and manage the police service.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The National Commissioner must exercise control over and manage the police service in accordance with the national policing policy and the directions of the Cabinet member responsible for policing.

      SUBSEC (3)

        The National Commissioner with the concurrence of the provincial executive, must appoint a woman or a man as the provincial commissioner for that province, but if the National Commissioner and the provincial executive are unable to agree on the appointment, the Cabinet member responsible for policing must mediate between the parties.

      SUBSEC (4)

        ITEMS
          The provincial commissioners are responsible for policing in their respective provinces -

          ITEM (a)
            as prescribed by national legislation; and

          ITEM (b)
            subject to the power of the National Commissioner to exercise control over and manage the police service in terms of subsection (2).

      SUBSEC (5)

        The provincial commissioner must report to the provincial legislature annually on policing in the province, and must send a copy of the report to the National Commissioner.

      SUBSEC (6)

        If the provincial commissioner has lost the confidence of the provincial executive, that executive may institute appropriate proceedings for the removal or transfer of, or disciplinary action against, that commissioner in accordance with national legislation.

    SEC 208. - Police civilian secretariat

      A civilian secretariat for the police service must be established by national legislation to function under the direction of the Cabinet member responsible for policing.

  SUBPART - Intelligence

    SEC 209. - Establishment and control of intelligence services

      SUBSEC (1)

        Any intelligence service, other than any intelligence division of the defence force or police service, may be established only by the President, as head of the national executive, and only in terms of national legislation.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The President as head of the national executive must appoint a woman or a man as head of each intelligence service established in terms of subsection (1), and must either assume political responsibility for the control and direction of any of those services, or designate a member of the Cabinet to assume that responsibility.

    SEC 210. - Powers, functions and monitoring

      ITEMS
        National legislation must regulate the objects, powers and functions of the intelligence services, including any intelligence division of the defence force or police service, and must provide for -

        ITEM (a)
          the co-ordination of all intelligence services; and

        ITEM (b)
          civilian monitoring of the activities of those services by an inspector appointed by the President, as head of the national executive, and approved by a resolution adopted by the National Assembly with a supporting vote of at least two thirds of its members.

CHAP 12 - Traditional leaders

  SEC 211. - Recognition

    SUBSEC (1)

      The institution, status and role of traditional leadership, according to customary law, are recognised, subject to the Constitution.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A traditional authority that observes a system of customary law may function subject to any applicable legislation and customs, which includes amendments to, or repeal of, that legislation or those customs.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The courts must apply customary law when that law is applicable, subject to the Constitution and any legislation that specifically deals with customary law.

  SEC 212. - Role of traditional leaders

    SUBSEC (1)

      National legislation may provide for a role for traditional leadership as an institution at local level on matters affecting local communities.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        To deal with matters relating to traditional leadership, the role of traditional leaders, customary law and the customs of communities observing a system of customary law -

        ITEM (a)
          national or provincial legislation may provide for the establishment of houses of traditional leaders; and

        ITEM (b)
          national legislation may establish a council of traditional leaders.

CHAP 13 - Finance

  SUBPART - General financial matters

    SEC 213. - National Revenue Fund

      SUBSEC (1)

        There is a National Revenue Fund into which all money received by the national government must be paid, except money reasonably excluded by an Act of Parliament.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          Money may be withdrawn from the National Revenue Fund only -

          ITEM (a)
            in terms of an appropriation by an Act of Parliament; or

          ITEM (b)
            as a direct charge against the National Revenue Fund, when it is provided for in the Constitution or an Act of Parliament.

      SUBSEC (3)

        A province’s equitable share of revenue raised nationally is a direct charge against the National Revenue Fund.

    SEC 214. - Equitable shares aid allocatings of revenue

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          An Act of Parliament must provide for -

          ITEM (a)
            the equitable division of revenue raised nationally among the national, provincial and local spheres of government;

          ITEM (b)
            the determination of each province’s equitable share of the provincial share of that revenue; and

          ITEM (c)
            any other allocations to provinces, local government or municipalities from the national government’s share of that revenue, and any conditions on which those allocations may be made.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The Act referred to in subsection (1) may be enacted only after the provincial governments, organised local government and the Financial and Fiscal Commission have been consulted, and any recommendations of the Commission have been considered, and must take into account -

          ITEM (a)
            the national interest;

          ITEM (b)
            any provision that must be made in respect of the national debt and other national obligations;

          ITEM (c)
            the needs and interests of the national government, determined by objective criteria;

          ITEM (d)
            the need to ensure that the provinces and municipalities are able to provide basic services and perform the functions allocated to them;

          ITEM (e)
            the fiscal capacity and efficiency of the provinces and municipalities;

          ITEM (f)
            developmental and other needs of provinces, local government and municipalities;

          ITEM (g)
            economic disparities within and among the provinces;

          ITEM (h)
            obligations of the provinces and municipalities in terms of national legislation;

          ITEM (i)
            the desirability of stable and predictable allocations of revenue shares; and

          ITEM (j)
            the need for flexibility in responding to emergencies or other temporary needs, and other factors based on similar objective criteria.

    SEC 215. - National, provincial and municipal budgets

      SUBSEC (1)

        National, provincial and municipal budgets and budgetary processes must promote transparency, accountability and the effective financial management of the economy, debt and the public sector.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          National legislation must prescribe -

          ITEM (a)
            the form of national, provincial and municipal budgets;

          ITEM (b)
            when national and provincial budgets must be tabled; and

          ITEM (c)
            that budgets in each sphere of government must show the sources of revenue and the way in which proposed expenditure will comply with national legislation.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          Budgets in each sphere of government must contain -

          ITEM (a)
            estimates of revenue and expenditure, differentiating between capital and current expenditure;

          ITEM (b)
            proposals for financing any anticipated deficit for the period to which they apply; and

          ITEM (c)
            an indication of intentions regarding borrowing and other forms of public liability that will increase public debt during the ensuing year.

    SEC 216. - Treasury control

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          National legislation must establish a national treasury and prescribe measures to ensure both transparency and expenditure control in each sphere of government, by introducing -

          ITEM (a)
            generally recognised accounting practice;

          ITEM (b)
            uniform expenditure classifications; and

          ITEM (c)
            uniform treasury norms and standards.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The national treasury, with the concurrence of the Cabinet member responsible for national financial matters, may stop the transfer of funds to an organ of state only for serious or persistent material breach of the measures established in terms of subsection (1).

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          A decision to stop the transfer of funds to a province may be taken only in terms of subsection (2), and -

          ITEM (a)
            may not stop the transfer of funds for more than 120 days; and

          ITEM (b)
            may be enforced immediately, but will lapse retrospectively unless Parliament approves it following a process substantially the same as that established in terms of {{>#sec_76 section 76}}(1) and prescribed by the joint rules and orders of Parliament. This process must be completed within 30 days of the decision by the national treasury.

      SUBSEC (4)

        Parliament may renew a decision to stop the transfer of funds for no more than 120 days at a time, following the process established in terms of subsection (3).

      SUBSEC (5)

        ITEMS
          Before Parliament may approve or renew a decision to stop the transfer of funds to a province -

          ITEM (a)
            the Auditor-General must report to Parliament; and

          ITEM (b)
            the province must be given an opportunity to answer the allegations against it, and to state its case, before a committee.

    SEC 217. - Procurement

      SUBSEC (1)

        When an organ of state in the national, provincial or local sphere of government, or any other institution identified in national legislation, contracts for goods or services, it must do so in accordance with a system which is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive and cost-effective.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          Subsection (1) does not prevent the organs of state or institutions referred to in that subsection from implementing a procurement policy providing for -

          ITEM (a)
            categories of preference in the allocation of contracts; and

          ITEM (b)
            the protection or advancement of persons, or categories of persons, disadvantaged by unfair discrimination.

      SUBSEC (3)

        National legislation must prescribe a framework within which the policy referred to in subsection (2) may be implemented.

    SEC 218. - Government guarantees

      SUBSEC (1)

        The national government, a provincial government or a municipality may guarantee a loan only if the guarantee complies with any conditions set out in national legislation.

      SUBSEC (2)

        National legislation referred to in subsection (1) may be enacted only after any recommendations of the Financial and Fiscal Commission have been considered.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Each year, every government must publish a report on the guarantees it has granted.

    SEC 219. - Remuneration of persons holding public office

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          An Act of Parliament must establish a framework for determining -

          ITEM (a)
            the salaries, allowances and benefits of members of the National Assembly, permanent delegates to the National Council of Provinces, members of the Cabinet, Deputy Ministers, traditional leaders and members of any councils of traditional leaders; and

          ITEM (b)
            the upper limit of salaries, allowances or benefits of members of provincial legislatures, members of Executive Councils and members of Municipal Councils of the different categories.

      SUBSEC (2)

        National legislation must establish an independent commission to make recommendations concerning the salaries, allowances and benefits referred to in subsection (1).

      SUBSEC (3)

        Parliament may pass the legislation referred to in subsection (1) only after considering any recommendations of the commission established in terms of subsection (2).

      SUBSEC (4)

        The national executive, a provincial executive, a municipality or any other relevant authority may implement the national legislation referred to in subsection (1) only after considering any recommendations of the commission established in terms of subsection (2).

      SUBSEC (5)

        National legislation must establish frameworks for determining the salaries, allowances and benefits of judges, the Public Protector, the Auditor-General, and members of any commission provided for in the Constitution, including the broadcasting authority referred to in {{>#sec_192 section 192}}.

  SUBPART - Financial and Fiscal Commission

    SEC 220. - Establishment and functions

      SUBSEC (1)

        There is a Financial and Fiscal Commission for the Republic which makes recommendations envisaged in this Chapter, or in national legislation, to Parliament, provincial legislatures and any other authorities determined by national legislation.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The Commission is independent and subject only to the Constitution and the law, and must be impartial.

      SUBSEC (3)

        The Commission must function in terms of an Act of Parliament and, in performing its functions, must consider all relevant factors, including those listed in {{>#sec_214 section 214}}(2).

    SEC 221. - Appointment and tenure of members

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The Commission consists of the following women and men appointed by the President, as head of the national executive:

          ITEM (a)
            A chairperson and a deputy chairperson, who are full-time members;

          ITEM (b)
            nine persons each of whom is nominated by the Executive Council of a province, with each province nominating only one person;

          ITEM (c)
            two persons nominated by organised local government in terms of {{>#sec_163 section 163}}; and

          ITEM (d)
            nine other persons.

      SUBSEC (2)

        Members of the Commission must have appropriate expertise.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Members serve for a term established in terms of national legislation. The President may remove a member from office on the ground of misconduct, incapacity or incompetence.

    SEC 222. - Reports

      The Commission must report regularly both to Parliament and to the provincial legislatures.

  SUBPART - Central bank

    SEC 223. - Establishment

      The South African Reserve Bank is the central bank of the Republic and is regulated in terms of an Act of Parliament.

    SEC 224. - Primary object

      SUBSEC (1)

        The primary object of the South African Reserve Bank is to protect the value of the currency in the interest of balanced and sustainable economic growth in the Republic.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The South African Reserve Bank, in pursuit of its primary object, must perform its functions independently and without fear, favour or prejudice, but there must be regular consultation between the Bank and the Cabinet member responsible for national financial matters.

    SEC 225. - Powers and functions

      The powers and functions of the South African Reserve Bank are those customarily exercised and performed by central banks, which powers and functions must be determined by an Act of Parliament and must be exercised or performed subject to the conditions prescribed in terms of that Act.

  SUBPART - Provincial and local financial matters

    SEC 226. - Provincial Revenue Funds

      SUBSEC (1)

        There is a Provincial Revenue Fund for each province into which all money received by the provincial government must be paid, except money reasonably excluded by an Act of Parliament.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          Money may be withdrawn from a Provincial Revenue Fund only -

          ITEM (a)
            in terms of an appropriation by a provincial Act; or

          ITEM (b)
            as a direct charge against the Provincial Revenue Fund, when it is provided for in the Constitution or a provincial Act.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Revenue allocated through a province to local government in that province in terms of {{>#sec_214 section 214}}(1), is a direct charge against that province’s Revenue Fund.

    SEC 227. - National sources of provincial and local government funding

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Local government and each province -

          ITEM (a)
            is entitled to an equitable share of revenue raised nationally to enable it to provide basic services and perform the functions allocated to it; and

          ITEM (b)
            may receive other allocations from national government revenue, either conditionally or unconditionally

      SUBSEC (2)

        Additional revenue raised by provinces or municipalities may not be deducted from their share of revenue raised nationally, or from other allocations made to them out of national government revenue. Equally, there is no obligation on the national government to compensate provinces or municipalities that do not raise revenue commensurate with their fiscal capacity and tax base.

      SUBSEC (3)

        A province’s equitable share of revenue raised nationally must be transferred to the province promptly and without deduction, except when the transfer has been stopped in terms of {{>#sec_216 section 216}}.

      SUBSEC (4)

        A province must provide for itself any resources that it requires, in terms of a provision of its provincial constitution, that are additional to its requirements envisaged in the Constitution.

    SEC 228. - Provincial taxes

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          A provincial legislature may impose —

          ITEM (a)
            taxes, levies and duties other than income tax, value-added tax, general sales tax, rates on property or customs duties; and

          ITEM (b)
            flat-rate surcharges on the tax bases of any tax, levy or duty that is imposed by national legislation, other than the tax bases of corporate income tax, value-added tax, rates on property or customs duties.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The power of a provincial legislature to impose taxes, levies, duties and surcharges —

          ITEM (a)
            may not be exercised in a way that materially and unreasonably prejudices national economic policies, economic activities across provincial boundaries, or the national mobility of goods, services, capital or labour; and

          ITEM (b)
            must be regulated in terms of an Act of Parliament, which may be enacted only after any recommendations of the Financial and Fiscal Commission have been considered.

    SEC 229. - Municipal fiscal powers and functions

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          Subject to subsections (2), (3) and (4), a municipality may impose -

          ITEM (a)
            rates on property and surcharges on fees for services provided by or on behalf of the municipality; and

          ITEM (b)
            if authorised by national legislation, other taxes, levies and duties appropriate to local government or to the category of local government into which that municipality falls, but no municipality may impose income tax, value-added tax, general sales tax or customs duty.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The power of a municipality to impose rates on property, surcharges on fees for services provided by or on behalf of the municipality, or other taxes, levies or duties -

          ITEM (a)
            may not be exercised in a way that materially and unreasonably prejudices national economic policies, economic activities across municipal boundaries, or the national mobility of goods, services, capital or labour; and

          ITEM (b)
            may be regulated by national legislation.

      SUBSEC (3)

        ITEMS
          When two municipalities have the same fiscal powers and functions with regard to the same area, an appropriate division of those powers and functions must be made in terms of national legislation. The division may be made only after taking into account at least the following criteria:

          ITEM (a)
            The need to comply with sound principles of taxation.

          ITEM (b)
            The powers and functions performed by each municipality.

          ITEM (c)
            The fiscal capacity of each municipality.

          ITEM (d)
            The effectiveness and efficiency of raising taxes, levies and duties.

          ITEM (e)
            Equity.

      SUBSEC (4)

        Nothing in this section precludes the sharing of revenue raised in terms of this section between municipalities that have fiscal power and functions in the same area.

      SUBSEC (5)

        National legislation envisaged in this section may be enacted only after organised local government and the Financial and Fiscal Commission have been consulted, and any recommendations of the Commission have been considered.

    SEC 230. - Provincial and municipal loans

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          A province or a municipality may raise loans for capital or current expenditure in accordance with reasonable conditions determined by national legislation, but loans for current expenditure -

          ITEM (a)
            may be raised only when necessary for bridging purposes during a fiscal year; and

          ITEM (b)
            must be repaid within twelve months.

      SUBSEC (2)

        National legislation referred to in subsection (1) may be enacted only after any recommendations of the Financial and Fiscal Commission have been considered.

CHAP 14 - General Provisions

  SUBPART - International law

    SEC 231. - International agreements

      SUBSEC (1)

        The negotiating and signing of all international agreements is the responsibility of the national executive.

      SUBSEC (2)

        An international agreement binds the Republic only after it has been approved by resolution in both the National Assembly and the National Council of Provinces, unless it is an agreement referred to in subsection (3).

      SUBSEC (3)

        An international agreement of a technical, administrative or executive nature, or an agreement which does not require either ratification or accession, entered into by the national executive, binds the Republic without approval by the National Assembly and the National Council of Provinces, but must be tabled in the Assembly and the Council within a reasonable time.

      SUBSEC (4)

        Any international agreement becomes law in the Republic when it is enacted into law by national legislation; but a self-executing provision of an agreement that has been approved by Parliament is law in the Republic unless it is inconsistent with the Constitution or an Act of Parliament.

      SUBSEC (5)

        The Republic is bound by international agreements which were binding on the Republic when this Constitution took effect.

    SEC 232. - Customary international law

      Customary international law is law in the Republic unless it is inconsistent with the Constitution or an Act of Parliament.

    SEC 233. - Application of international law

      When interpreting any legislation, every court must prefer any reasonable interpretation of the legislation that is consistent with international law over any alternative interpretation that is inconsistent with intemational law.

  SUBPART - Other matters

    SEC 234. - Charters of Rights

      In order to deepen the culture of democracy established by the  Constitution, Parliament may adopt Charters of Rights consistent with the provisions of the Constitution.

    SEC 235. - Self-determination

      The right of the South African people as a whole to self-determination, as manifested in this Constitution, does not preclude, within the framework of this right, recognition of the notion of the right of self-determination of any community sharing a common cultural and language heritage, within a territorial entity in the Republic or in any other way, determined by national legislation.

    SEC 236. - Funding for political parties

      To enhance multi-party democracy, national legislation must provide for the funding of political parties participating in national and provincial legislatures on an equitable and proportional basis.

    SEC 237. - Diligent performance of obligations

      All constitutional obligations must be performed diligently and without delay.

    SEC 238. - Agency and delegation

      ITEMS
        An executive organ of state in any sphere of government may -

        ITEM (a)
          delegate any power or function that is to be exercised or performed in terms of legislation to any other executive organ of state, provided the delegation is consistent with the legislation in terms of which the power is exercised or the function is performed; or

        ITEM (b)
          exercise any power or perform any function for any other executive organ of state on an agency or delegation basis.

    SEC 239. - Definitions

      In the Constitution, unless the context indicates otherwise -

      ITEMS
        "national legislation" includes -

        ITEM (a)
          subordinate legislation made in terms of an Act of Parliament; and

        ITEM (b)
          legislation that was in force when the Constitution took effect and that is administered by the national government;

      ITEMS
        "organ of state" means -

        ITEM (a)
          any department of state or administration in the national, provincial or local sphere of government; or

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            any other functionary or institution -

            ITEM (i)
              exercising a power or performing a function in terms of the Constitution or a provincial constitution; or

            ITEM (ii)
              exercising a public power or performing a public function in terms of any legislation,

      but does not include a court or a judicial officer;

      ITEMS
        "provincial legislation" includes -

        ITEM (a)
          subordinate legislation made in terms of a provincial Act; and

        ITEM (b)
          legislation that was in force when the Constitution  took effect and that is administered by a provincial government.

    SEC 240. - Inconsistencies between different texts

      In the event of an inconsistency between different texts of the Constitution, the English text prevails.

    SEC 241. - Transitional arrangements

      {{>#att_7 Schedule 6}} applies to the transition to the new constitutional order established by this Constitution, and any matter incidental to that transition.

    SEC 242. - Repeal of laws

      The laws mentioned in {{>#att_10 Schedule 7}} are repealed, subject to {{>#sec_243 section 243}} and {{>#att_7 Schedule 6}}.

    SEC 243. - Short title and commencement

      SUBSEC (1)

        This Act is called the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, and comes into effect as soon as possible on a date set by the President by proclamation, which may not be a date later than 1 July 1997.

      SUBSEC (2)

        The President may set different dates before the date mentioned in subsection (1) in respect of different provisions of the Constitution.

      SUBSEC (3)

        Unless the context otherwise indicates, a reference in a provision of the Constitution  to a time when the Constitution  took effect must be construed as a reference to the time when that provision took effect.

      SUBSEC (4)

        If a different date is set for any particular provision of the Constitution  in terms of subsection (2), any corresponding provision of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1993 ({{>/akn/za/act/1993/200 Act 200 of 1993}}), mentioned in the proclamation, is repealed with effect from the same date.

      SUBSEC (5)

        Sections {{>#sec_213 213}}, {{>#sec_214 214}}, {{>#sec_215 215}}, {{>#sec_216 216}}, {{>#sec_218 218}}, {{>#sec_226 226}}, {{>#sec_227 227}}, {{>#sec_228 228}}, {{>#sec_229 229}} and {{>#sec_230 230}} come into effect on 1 January 1998, but this does not preclude the enactment in terms of this Constitution  of legislation envisaged in any of these provisions before that date. Until that date any corresponding and incidental provisions of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1993, remain in force.

SCHEDULE Schedule 1
  SUBHEADING National flag

  SUBSEC (1)

    The national flag is rectangular; it is one and a half times longer than it is wide.

  SUBSEC (2)

    It is black, gold, green, white, chilli red and blue.

  SUBSEC (3)

    It has a green Y-shaped band that is one fifth as wide as the flag. The centre lines of the band start in the top and bottom comers next to the flag post, converge in the centre of the flag, and continue horizontally to the middle of the free edge.

  SUBSEC (4)

    The green band is edged, above and below in white, and towards the flag post end, in gold. Each edging is one fifteenth as wide as the flag.

  SUBSEC (5)

    The triangle next to the flag post is black.

  SUBSEC (6)

    The upper horizontal band is chilli red and the lower horizontal band is blue. These bands are each one third as wide as the flag.

SCHEDULE Schedule 2
  SUBHEADING Oaths and solemn affirmations

  SEC 1. - Oath or solemn affirmation of President and Acting President

    The President or Acting President, before the President of the Constitutional Court, or another judge designated by the President of the Constitutional Court, must swear/affirm as follows:

    [[introductory words substituted by section 2 of the {{>/akn/za/act/1997/constitution-first-amendment Constitution First Amendment Act, 1997}}]]

    In the presence of everyone assembled here, and in full realisation of the high calling I assume as President/Acting President of the Republic of South Africa, I, A.B., swear/solemnly affirm that I will be faithful to the Republic of South Africa, and will obey, observe, uphold and maintain the Constitution and all other law of the Republic; and I solemnly and sincerely promise that I will always -

    □ promote all that will advance the Republic, and oppose all that may harm it;

    □ protect and promote the rights of all South Africans;

    □ discharge my duties with all my strength and talents to the best of my knowledge and ability and true to the dictates of my conscience;

    □ do justice to all; and

    □ devote myself to the well-being of the Republic and all of its people.

    \(In the case of an oath: So help me God.)

  SEC 2. - Oath or solemn affirmation of Deputy President

    The Deputy President, before the President of the Constitutional Court, must swear/affirm as follows:

    In the presence of everyone assembled here, and in full realisation of the high calling I assume as Deputy President of the Republic of South Africa, I, A.B.,   swear/solemnly affirm that I will be faithful to the Republic of South Africa and will obey, observe, uphold and maintain the Constitution and all other law of the Republic; and I solemnly and sincerely promise that I will always -

    □ promote all that will advance the Republic, and oppose all that may harm it;

    □ be a true and faithful counsellor;

    □ discharge my duties with all my strength and talents to the best of my knowledge and ability and true to the dictates of my conscience;

    □ do justice to all; and

    □ devote myself to the well-being of the Republic and all of its people.

    \(In the case of an oath: So help me God.)

  SEC 3. - Oath or solemn affirmation of Ministers and Deputy Ministers

    Each Minister and Deputy Minister, before the President of the Constitutional Court or another judge designated by the President of the Constitutional Court, must swear/affirm as follows:

    I, A.B., swear/solemnly affirm that I will be faithful to the Republic of South Africa and will obey, respect and uphold the Constitution and all other law of the Republic; and I undertake to hold my office as Minister/Deputy Minister with honour and dignity; to be a true and faithful counsellor; not to divulge directly or indirectly any secret matter entrusted to me; and to perform the functions of my office conscientiously and to the best of my ability.

    \(In the case of an oath: So help me God.)

  SEC 4. - Oath or solemn affirmation of members of the National Assembly, permanent delegates to the National Council of Provinces and members of the provincial legislatures

    SUBSEC (1)

      Members of the National Assembly, permanent delegates to the National Council of Provinces and members of provincial legislatures, before the President of the Constitutional Court or a judge designated by the President of the Constitutional Court, must swear or affirm as follows:

      I, A.B., swear/solemnly affirm that I will be faithful to the Republic of South Africa and will obey, respect and uphold the Constitution and all other law of the Republic; and I solemnly promise to perform my functions as a member of the National Assembly/ permanent delegate to the National Council of Provinces/member of the legislature of the province of C.D. to the best of my ability.

      \(In the case of an oath: So help me God.)

    SUBSEC (2)

      Persons filling a vacancy in the National Assembly, a permanent delegation to the National Council of Provinces or a provincial legislature may swear or affirm in terms of subitem (1) before the presiding officer of the Assembly, Council or legislature, as the case may be.

  SEC 5. - Oath or solemn affirmation of Premiers, Acting Premiers and members of provincial Executive Councils

    The Premier or Acting Premier of a province, and each member of the Executive Council of a province, before the President of the Constitutional Court or a judge designated by the President of the Constitutional Court, must swear/affirm as follows:

    I, A.B., swear/solemnly affirm that I will be faithful to the Republic of South Africa and will obey, respect and uphold the Constitution and all other law of the Republic; and I undertake to hold my office as Premier/Acting Premier/member of the Executive Council of the province of C.D. with honour and dignity; to be a true and faithful counsellor; not to divulge directly or indirectly any secret matter entrusted to me; and to perform the functions of my office conscientiously and to the best of my ability.

    \(In the case of an oath: So help me God.)

  SEC 6. - Oath or solemn affirmation of judicial officers

    SUBSEC (1)

      Each judge or acting judge, before the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court of Appeal or another judge designated by the Chief Justice, must swear or affirm as follows:

      I, A.B., swear/solemnly affirm that, as a Judge of the Constitutional Court/Supreme Court of Appeal/High Court/ E.F. Court, I will be faithful to the Republic of South Africa, will uphold and protect the Constitution and the human rights entrenched in it, and will administer justice to all persons alike without fear, favour or prejudice, in accordance with the Constitution and the law.

      \(In the case of an oath: So help me God.)

    SUBSEC (2)

      A person appointed to the office of Chief Justice of the Supreme Court of Appeal who is not already a judge at the time of that appointment must swear or affirm before the President of the Constitutional Court.

    SUBSEC (3)

      Judicial officers, and acting judicial officers, other than judges, must swear/affirm in terms of national legislation.

SCHEDULE Schedule 3
  SUBHEADING Election Procedures

  PART A - Election procedures for constitutional office-bearers

    SEC 1. - Application

      ITEMS
        The procedure set out in this Schedule applies whenever -

        ITEM (a)
          the National Assembly meets to elect the President, or the Speaker or Deputy Speaker of the Assembly;

        ITEM (b)
          the National Council of Provinces meets to elect its Chairperson or a Deputy Chairperson; or

        ITEM (c)
          a provincial legislature meets to elect the Premier of the province or the Speaker or Deputy Speaker of the legislature.

    SEC 2. - Nominations

      The person presiding at a meeting to which this Schedule applies must call for the nomination of candidates at the meeting.

    SEC 3. - Formal requirements

      SUBSEC (1)

        A nomination must be made on the form prescribed by the rules mentioned in item 9.

      SUBSEC (2)

        ITEMS
          The form on which a nomination is made must be signed -

          ITEM (a)
            by two members of the National Assembly, if the President or the Speaker or Deputy Speaker of the Assembly is to be elected;

          ITEM (b)
            on behalf of two provincial delegations, if the Chairperson or a Deputy Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces is to be elected; or

          ITEM (c)
            by two members of the relevant provincial legislature, if the Premier of the province or the Speaker or Deputy Speaker of the legislature is to be elected.

      SUBSEC (3)

        A person who is nominated must indicate acceptance of the nomination by signing either the nomination form or any other form of written confirmation.

    SEC 4. - Announcement of names of candidates

      At a meeting to which this Schedule applies, the person presiding must announce the names of the persons who have been nominated as candidates, but may not permit any debate.

    SEC 5. - Single candidate

      If only one candidate is nominated, the person presiding must declare that candidate elected.

    SEC 6. - Election procedure

      ITEMS
        If more than one candidate is nominated -

        ITEM (a)
          a vote must be taken at the meeting by secret ballot;

        ITEM (b)
          each member present, or if it is a meeting of the National Council of Provinces, each province represented, at the meeting may cast one vote; and

        ITEM (c)
          the person presiding must declare elected the candidate who receives a majority of the votes.

    SEC 7. - Elimination procedure

      SUBSEC (1)

        If no candidate receives a majority of the votes, the candidate who receives the lowest number of votes must be eliminated and a further vote taken on the remaining candidates in accordance with item 6. This procedure must be repeated until a candidate receives a majority of the votes.

      SUBSEC (2)

        When applying subitem (1), if two or more candidates each have the lowest number of votes, a separate vote must be taken on those candidates, and repeated as often as may be necessary to determine which candidate is to be eliminated.

    SEC 8. - Further meetings

      SUBSEC (1)

        If only two candidates are nominated, or if only two candidates remain after an elimination procedure has been applied, and those two candidates receive the same number of votes, a further meeting must be held within seven days, at a time determined by the person presiding.

      SUBSEC (2)

        If a further meeting is held in terms of subitem (1), the procedure prescribed in this Schedule must be applied at that meeting as if it were the first meeting for the election in question.

    SEC 9. - Rules

      SUBSEC (1)

        ITEMS
          The President of the Constitutional Court must make rules prescribing -

          ITEM (a)
            the procedure for meetings to which this Schedule applies;

          ITEM (b)
            the duties of any person presiding at a meeting, and of any person assisting the person presiding;

          ITEM (c)
            the form on which nominations must be submitted; and

          ITEM (d)
            the manner in which voting is to be conducted.

      SUBSEC (2)

        These rules must be made known in the way that the President of the Constitutional Court determines.

  PART B - Formula to determine party participation in provincial delegations to the National Council of Provinces

    PARA 1.

      The number of delegates in a provincial delegation to the National Council of Provinces to which a party is entitled, must be determined by multiplying the number of seats the party holds in the provincial legislature by ten and dividing the result by the number of seats in the legislature plus one.

    PARA 2.

      If a calculation in terms of item 1 yields a surplus not absorbed by the delegates allocated to a party in terms of that item, the surplus must compete with similar surpluses accruing to any other party or parties, and any undistributed delegates in the delegation must be allocated to the party or parties in the sequence of the highest surplus.

SCHEDULE Schedule 6
  SUBHEADING Transitional arrangements

  SEC 1. - Definitions

    In this Schedule, unless inconsistent with the context —

    "homeland" means a part of the Republic which, before the previous Constitution took effect, was dealt with in South African legislation as an independent or a self-governing territory;

    "new Constitution " means the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996;

    "old order legislation" means legislation enacted before the previous Constitution took effect;

    "previous Constitution" means the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1993 ({{>/akn/za/act/1993/200 Act 200 of 1993}}).

  SEC 2. - Continuation of existing law

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        All law that was in force when the new Constitution took effect, continues in force, subject to -

        ITEM (a)
          any amendment or repeal; and

        ITEM (b)
          consistency with the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Old order legislation that continues in force in terms of subitem (1) -

        ITEM (a)
          does not have a wider application, territorially or otherwise, than it had before the previous Constitution took effect unless subsequently amended to have a wider application; and

        ITEM (b)
          continues to be administered by the authorities that administered it when the new Constitution took effect, subject to the new Constitution.

  SEC 3. - Interpretation of existing legislation

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Unless inconsistent with the context or clearly inappropriate, a reference in any legislation that existed when the new Constitution took effect -

        ITEM (a)
          to the Republic of South Africa or a homeland (except when it refers to a territorial area), must be construed as a reference to the Republic of South Africa under the new Constitution;

        ITEM (b)
          to Parliament, the National Assembly or the Senate, must be construed as a reference to Parliament, the National Assembly or the National Council of Provinces under the new Constitution;

        ITEM (c)
          to the President, an Executive Deputy President, a Minister a reference to the President, the Deputy President, a Minister, a Deputy Minister or the Cabinet under the new Constitution, subject to item 9 of this Schedule;

        ITEM (d)
          to the President of the Senate, must be construed as a reference to the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces;

        ITEM (e)
          to a provincial legislature, Premier, Executive Council or member of an Executive Council of a province, must be construed as a reference to a provincial legislature, Premier, Executive Council or member of an Executive Council under the new Constitution, subject to item 12 of this Schedule; or

        ITEM (f)
          to an official language or languages, must be construed as a reference to any of the official languages under the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        Unless inconsistent with the context or clearly inappropriate, a reference in any remaining old order legislation -

        ITEM (a)
          ITEMS
            to a Parliament, a House of a Parliament or a legislative assembly or body of the Republic or of a homeland, must be construed as a reference to -

            ITEM (i)
              Parliament under the new Constitution, if the administration of that legislation has been allocated or assigned in terms of the previous Constitution or this Schedule to the national executive; or

            ITEM (ii)
              the provincial legislature of a province, if the administration of that legislation has been allocated or assigned in terms of the previous Constitution or this Schedule to a provincial executive; or

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            to a State President, Chief Minister, Administrator or other chief executive, Cabinet, Ministers’ Council or executive council of the Republic or of a homeland, must be construed as a reference to -

            ITEM (i)
              the President under the new Constitution, if the administration of that legislation has been allocated or assigned in terms of the previous Constitution or this Schedule to the national executive; or

            ITEM (ii)
              the Premier of a province under the new Constitution, if the administration of that legislation has been allocated or assigned in terms of the previous Constitution or this Schedule to a provincial executive.

  SEC 4. - National Assembly

    SUBSEC (1)

      Anyone who was a member or office-bearer of the National Assembly when the new Constitution took effect, becomes a member or office-bearer of the National Assembly under the new Constitution, and holds office as a member or office-bearer in terms of the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (2)

      The National Assembly as constituted in terms of subitem (1) must be regarded as having been elected under the new Constitution for a term that expires on 30 April 1999.

    SUBSEC (3)

      The National Assembly consists of 400 members for the duration of its term that expires on 30 April 1999, subject to {{>#sec_49 section 49}}(4) of the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (4)

      The rules and orders of the National Assembly in force when the new Constitution took effect, continue in force, subject to any amendment or repeal.

  SEC 5. - Unfinished business before Parliament

    SUBSEC (1)

      Any unfinished business before the National Assembly when the new Constitution takes effect must be proceeded with in terms of the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Any unfinished business before the Senate when the new Constitution takes effect must be referred to the National Council of Provinces, and the Council must proceed with that business in terms of the new Constitution.

  SEC 6. - Elections of National Assembly

      No election of the National Assembly may be held before 30 April 1999 unless the Assembly is dissolved in terms of {{>#sec_50 section 50}}(2) after a motion of no confidence in the President in terms of {{>#sec_102 section 102}}(2) of the new Constitution.

        ITEM (c)
          to the filling of vacancies in the Assembly, and the supplementation, review and use of party lists for the filling of vacancies, until the second election of the Assembly under the new Constitution.

  SEC 8. - Former senators

    SUBSEC (1)

      A former senator who is not appointed as a permanent delegate to the National Council of Provinces is entitled to become a full voting member of the legislature of the province from which that person was nominated as a senator in terms of section 48 of the previous Constitution.

  SEC 9. - National executive

    SUBSEC (1)

    SUBSEC (3)

      Subitem (2) does not prevent a Minister who was a senator when the new Constitution took effect, from continuing as a Minister referred to in {{>#sec_91 section 91}}(1)(a) of the new Constitution, as that section reads in Annexure B.

  SEC 10. - Provincial legislature

    SUBSEC (1)

      Anyone who was a member or office-bearer of a province’s legislature when the new Constitution took effect, becomes a member or office-bearer of the legislature for that province under the new Constitution, and holds office as a member or office-bearer in terms of the new Constitution and any provincial constitution that may be enacted.

    SUBSEC (2)

      A provincial legislature as constituted in terms of subitem (1) must be regarded as having been elected under the new Constitution for a term that expires on 30 April 1999.

    SUBSEC (3)

      For the duration of its term that expires on 30 April 1999, and subject to {{>#sec_108 section 108}}(4), a provincial legislature consists of the number of members determined for that legislature under the previous Constitution plus the number of former senators who became members of the legislature in terms of item 8 of this Schedule.

    SUBSEC (4)

      The rules and orders of a provincial legislature in force when the new Constitution took effect, continue in force, subject to any amendment or repeal.

  SEC 11. - Elections of provincial legislatures

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Despite the repeal of the previous Constitution, Schedule 2 to that Constitution, as amended by Annexure A to this Schedule, applies -

        ITEM (a)
          to the first election of a provincial legislature under the new Constitution;

        ITEM (b)
          to the loss of membership of a legislature in circumstances other than those provided for in {{>#sec_106 section 106}}(3) of the new Constitution; and

        ITEM (c)
          to the filling of vacancies in a legislature, and the supplementation, review and use of party lists for the filling of vacancies, until the second election of the legislature under the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (2)

      {{>#sec_106 Section 106}}(4) of the new Constitution is suspended in respect of a provincial legislature until the second election of the legislature under the new Constitution.

  SEC 12. - Provincial executives

    SUBSEC (1)

    SUBSEC (2)

  SEC 13. - Provincial constitutions

    A provincial constitution passed before the new Constitution took effect must comply with {{>#sec_143 section 143}} of the new Constitution.

  SEC 14. - Assignment of legislation to provinces

    SUBSEC (1)

      Legislation with regard to a matter within a functional area listed in Schedule {{>#att_5 4}} or {{>#att_6 5}} to the new Constitution and which, when the new Constitution took effect, was administered by an authority within the national executive, may be assigned by the President, by proclamation, to an authority within a provincial executive designated by the Executive Council of the province.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        To the extent that it is necessary for an assignment of legislation under subitem (1) to be effectively carried out, the President, by proclamation, may -

        ITEM (a)
          amend or adapt the legislation to regulate its interpretation or application;

        ITEM (b)
          where the assignment does not apply to the whole of any piece of legislation, repeal and re-enact, with or without any amendments or adaptations referred to in paragraph (a), those provisions to which the assignment applies or to the extent that the assignment applies to them; or

        ITEM (c)
          regulate any other matter necessary as a result of the assignment, including the transfer or secondment of staff, or the transfer of assets, liabilities, rights and obligations, to or from the national or a provincial executive or any department of state, administration, security service or other institution.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          A copy of each proclamation issued in terms of subitem (1) or (2) must be submitted to the National Assembly and the National Council of Provinces within 10 days of the publication of the proclamation.

        ITEM (b)
          ITEMS
            If both the National Assembly and the National Council by resolution disapprove the proclamation or any provision of it, the proclamation or provision lapses, but without affecting —

            ITEM (i)
              the validity of anything done in terms of the proclamation or provision before it lapsed; or

            ITEM (ii)
              a right or privilege acquired or an obligation or liability incurred before it lapsed.

    SUBSEC (4)

      When legislation is assigned under subitem (1), any reference in the legislation to an authority administering it, must be construed as a reference to the authority to which it has been assigned.

    SUBSEC (5)

      Any assignment of legislation under section 235(8) of the previous Constitution, including any amendment, adaptation or repeal and re-enactment of any legislation and any other action taken under that section, is regarded as having been done under this item.

  SEC 15. - Existing legislation outside Parliament's legislative power

    SUBSEC (1)

      An authority within the national executive that administers any legislation falling outside Parliament’s legislative power when the new Constitution  takes effect, remains competent to administer that legislation until it is assigned to an authority within a provincial executive in terms of item 14 of this Schedule.

    SUBSEC (2)

      Subitem (1) lapses two years after the new Constitution took effect.

  SEC 16. - Courts

    SUBSEC (1)

      ITEMS
        Every court, including courts of traditional leaders, existing when the new Constitution took effect, continues to function and to exercise jurisdiction in terms of the legislation applicable to it, and anyone holding office as a judicial officer continues to hold office in terms of the legislation applicable to that office, subject to —

        ITEM (a)
          any amendment or repeal of that legislation; and

        ITEM (b)
          consistency with the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (2)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          The Constitutional Court established by the previous Constitution becomes the Constitutional Court under the new Constitution.

        ITEM (b)
          Anyone holding office as the President, the Deputy President or a judge of the Constitutional Court when the new Constitution takes effect, becomes the President, the Deputy President or a judge of the Constitutional Court under the new Constitution, and continues in office for the unexpired portion of their term as fixed by {{>#sec_176 section 176}}(1) of the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (3)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          The Appellate Division of the Supreme Court of South Africa becomes the Supreme Court of Appeal under the new Constitution.

        ITEM (b)
          Anyone holding office as the Chief Justice, the Deputy Chief Justice or a judge of the Appellate Division when the new Constitution takes effect, becomes the Chief Justice, the Deputy Chief Justice or a judge of the Supreme Court of Appeal under the new Constitution.

    SUBSEC (4)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          A provincial or local division of the Supreme Court of South Africa or a supreme court of a homeland or a general division of such a court, becomes a High Court under the new Constitution without any alteration in its area of jurisdiction, subject to any rationalisation contemplated in subitem (6).

        ITEM (b)
          Anyone holding office or deemed to hold office as the Judge President, the Deputy Judge President or a judge of a court referred to in paragraph (a) when the new Constitution takes effect, becomes the Judge President, the Deputy Judge President or a judge of such a court under the new Constitution, subject to any rationalisation contemplated in subitem (6).

    SUBSEC (5)

      ITEMS
        Unless inconsistent with the context or clearly inappropriate, a reference in any legislation or process to -

        ITEM (a)
          the Constitutional Court under the previous Constitution, must be construed as a reference to the Constitutional Court under the new Constitution;

        ITEM (b)
          the Appellate Division of the Supreme Court of South Africa, must be construed as a reference to the Supreme Court of Appeal; and

        ITEM (c)
          a provincial or local division of the Supreme Court of South Africa or a supreme court of a homeland or general division of that court, must be construed as a reference to a High Court.

    SUBSEC (6)

      ITEMS
        ITEM (a)
          As soon as is practical after the new Constitution took effect all courts, including their structure, composition, functioning and jurisdiction, and all relevant legislation, must be rationalised with a view to establishing a judicial system suited to the requirements of the new Constitution.

        ITEM (b)
          The Cabinet member responsible for the administration of justice, acting after consultation with the Judicial Service Commission, must manage the rationalisation envisaged in paragraph (a).

  SEC 17. - Cases pending before courts

    All proceedings which were pending before a court when the new Constitution took effect, must be disposed of as if the new Constitution had not been enacted, unless the interests of justice require otherwise.

  SEC 18. - Prosecuting authority

    SUBSEC (1)

      Section 108 of the previous Constitution continues in force until the Act of Parliament envisaged in {{>#sec_179 section 179}} of the new Constitution  takes effect. This subitem does not affect the appointment of the National Director of Public Prosecutions in terms of {{>#sec_179 section 179}}.

    SUBSEC (2)

              determine the specific portfolios to be allocated to the respective participating parties in accordance with the number of portfolios allocated to them in terms of subsection (3);

            ITEM (b)
              appoint in respect of each such portfolio a member of the provincial legislature who is a member of the party to which that portfolio was allocated under paragraph (a), as the member of the Executive Council responsible for that portfolio;

            ITEM (c)
              if it becomes necessary for the purposes of the Constitution or in the interest of good government, vary any determination under paragraph (a), subject to subsection (3);

            ITEM (d)
              ITEMS
                terminate any appointment under paragraph (b) -

                ITEM (i)
                  if the Premier is requested to do so by the leader of the party of which the Executive Council member in question is a member; or

                ITEM (ii)
                  if it becomes necessary for the purposes of the Constitution or in the interest of good government; or

            ITEM (e)
              fill, when necessary, subject to paragraph (b), a vacancy in the office of a member of the Executive Council.

        ITEM (5)
          ITEMS
            Subsection (4) must be implemented in the spirit embodied in the concept of a government of national unity, and the Premier and the other functionaries concerned must in the implementation of that subsection seek to achieve consensus at all times: Provided that if consensus cannot be achieved on -

            ITEM (a)
              the exercise of a power referred to in paragraph (a), (c) or (d)(ii) of that subsection, the Premier’s decision prevails;

            ITEM (b)
              the exercise of a power referred to in paragraph (b), (d)(i) or (e) of that subsection affecting a person who is not a member of the Premier’s party, the decision of the leader of the party of which such person is a member prevails; and

            ITEM (c)
              the exercise of a power referred to in paragraph (b) or (e) of that subsection affecting a person who is a member of the Premier’s party, the Premier’s decision prevails.

        ITEM (6)
          If any determination of portfolio allocations is varied under subsection (4)(c), the affected members must vacate their portfolios but are eligible, where applicable, for reappointment to other portfolios allocated to their respective parties in terms of the varied determination.

        ITEM (7)
          Meetings of an Executive Council must be presided over by the Premier of the province.

        ITEM (8)
          An Executive Council must function in a manner which gives consideration to the consensus-seeking spirit embodied in the concept of a government of national unity, as well as the need for effective government.".
